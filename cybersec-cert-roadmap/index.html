PART 1

Why Cybersecurity? The Landscape in 2026

Context for Leaving Cert students, BSc applicants, and recent graduates

Cybercrime is now the world's third-largest economy. If it were a country, its GDP would trail only the United States and China. That is not a metaphor: it is the scale of the problem that cybersecurity professionals exist to counter.

$10.5T
Global cybercrime cost in 2025, rising to $12.2T by 2031. 15% annual growth rate. (Cybersecurity Ventures)
4.8M
Global cybersecurity workforce gap. 5.5M active professionals, but 4.8M more are needed. 90% of organisations report skills shortages. (ISC2 2025 Workforce Study)
$4.88M
Average cost of a single data breach in 2024 (record high, +10% YoY). Organisations using AI-driven security cut this to $4.44M. (IBM Cost of a Data Breach 2024/2025)
$272B
Global cybersecurity market in 2025, growing 9-13% annually. Projected $352B by 2030. (Grand View Research, MarketsandMarkets)
4,701
Ransomware incidents in 2025 (+46% YoY). Average ransom payment: $1.13M. AI has cut attacker dwell time from 9 to 5 days. (Emsisoft)
14x
Surge in AI-generated phishing since late 2024. 50% of phishing is now AI-crafted. Deepfake files grew from 500K (2023) to 8M (2025). (Keepnet, ZeroThreat)

The bottom line: every organisation on earth needs cybersecurity talent, supply is nowhere near demand, and the gap is widening. This is not a bubble; it is a structural shift driven by the digitisation of everything from hospitals to power grids.

Europe is responding to this threat with the most ambitious cybersecurity regulatory programme in history. For job seekers, regulation equals demand: every new compliance requirement creates roles for the people who implement, audit, and maintain it.

160,000
Entities now in scope under NIS2 across the EU (up from ~10,000 under NIS1). 18 sectors. 22 of 27 member states have transposed the directive. (EU Digital Strategy)
299,000
Skilled cybersecurity professionals deficit across the EU. The gap is structural, not cyclical. (ENISA / ISC2)
€67B
European cybersecurity market in 2025, growing 12.4% annually. Projected €172B by 2033. (Statista, CBI)
€5.88B
Total GDPR fines issued across the EU since 2018. Ireland's DPC alone levied €652M in 2024 (over half of all EU fines that year). (Law Society Gazette, RTE)
Key EU Regulations Creating Cybersecurity Demand

NIS2 Directive (Oct 2024): Mandatory cybersecurity risk management for essential and important entities across 18 sectors.
DORA (Jan 2025): Digital Operational Resilience Act covering all EU financial entities (banks, insurers, crypto providers, ICT suppliers).
EU Cyber Solidarity Act (Feb 2025): Pan-European SOC network and EU Cybersecurity Reserve of trusted incident-response providers.
EU Cybersecurity Skills Academy: Commission-backed initiative to close the 299,000-person gap through funded training and certification programmes.

Ireland punches far above its weight in cybersecurity. As the European headquarters for most of the world's largest tech companies, and the GDPR lead regulator for 87% of cross-border complaints, Ireland sits at the epicentre of European digital security.

632
Cybersecurity firms across the island of Ireland. 399 in Dublin. 41% are multinational-headquartered. 13.4% average annual revenue growth. (Cyber Ireland All-Island Report 2025)
10,600+
Cybersecurity professionals employed in Ireland, generating €3.2B in revenue and €1.5B in gross value added. Target: 17,000 by 2030. (Cyber Ireland / IDA)
4,000+
Irish organisations entering NIS2 scope when the Network and Information System Security Act 2026 takes effect on 1 October 2026. Entity registration opens July 2026. (NCSC Ireland)
€100M+
Recovery cost of the 2021 HSE ransomware attack (Conti). Ireland's largest ever cyber incident. A reminder that this is not abstract: it shut down hospital systems nationwide. (Irish Examiner)

Who operates security teams from Ireland? Google, Microsoft, Apple, Meta, TikTok (ByteDance), Accenture Security, IBM, Mastercard (cyber fusion centre), Salesforce, Trend Micro, McAfee, and hundreds more. The Big Four (PwC, Deloitte, EY, KPMG) all run cybersecurity practices from Dublin.

Ireland as GDPR gatekeeper. The Data Protection Commission is the lead supervisory authority for 87% of cross-border GDPR complaints because most US tech firms have their EU headquarters here. That regulatory weight creates a permanent demand for data protection and security professionals in Ireland that does not exist elsewhere in Europe at the same density.

NCSC expansion. Post-HSE attack, the government doubled the NCSC director's salary, pledged 45 additional staff, and increased the budget from €4M (2020) to €6.9M. The NCSC is now the designated NIS2 competent authority.

1. The AI Arms Race
Voice cloning from 15 seconds of audio. 50% of phishing now AI-generated. Deepfake fraud up 2,137% over three years. Attackers are faster, but defenders who understand AI tools (Security Copilot, Sentinel AI) are in massive demand.
2. NIS2 Compliance Wave
160,000 EU entities and 4,000+ Irish organisations must implement cybersecurity risk management programmes. This is not optional: fines run up to €10M or 2% of global turnover. Every entity needs people to do this work.
3. DORA in Financial Services
Since January 2025, every EU bank, insurer, investment firm, and crypto provider must have ICT resilience frameworks. Ireland's IFSC (International Financial Services Centre) is one of Europe's largest, creating concentrated demand.
4. Cloud + Remote Work
Hybrid work and cloud migration have permanently expanded the attack surface. Every company running Azure, AWS, or GCP needs security engineers who understand cloud-native threats. This is now the default, not a speciality.
5. Quantum on the Horizon
NIST published post-quantum cryptography standards in 2024. The migration window is now: organisations must begin transitioning encryption before quantum computers break current standards. This creates a new speciality from scratch.
6. Critical Infrastructure
The HSE attack proved that cyber threats have real-world consequences: hospitals shut down, patients affected. As energy, transport, water, and health systems digitise, OT/ICS security becomes a career in its own right.
Leaving Cert Students
Choosing a degree? Cybersecurity, computer science with a security specialism, or network engineering are strong paths. Look for programmes with industry placements (TU Dublin, MTU, UL, UCD). The field rewards curiosity and problem-solving more than rote learning.
BSc Graduates (CS / IT / Engineering)
You already have the technical foundation. What you need now is signalling: certifications that prove your skills to recruiters, hands-on lab experience (TryHackMe, Hack The Box), and a first role. Part 2 of this guide is built specifically for you.
Career Changers
Coming from IT support, networking, development, or a non-tech background? Springboard+ offers 90-100% funded conversion courses. The ISC2 CC cert is free. Security+ is the universal entry point. Start there, get a role, then specialise.

Regardless of your starting point, the playbook is the same: build a foundation, get certified, get hired, then specialise. The market has room for all of you. The 4.8 million global workforce gap is not closing any time soon.

Progression pathways (Level 7 → Level 8 add-on years)

TU758 → TU863 (TU Dublin) • MT705 → MT805 (MTU Cork) • SE612 → SE606 (SETU Carlow) • AU262 → AU361 (ATU Donegal). These structured pathways let students enter at Level 7 and top up to an Honours degree in one additional year.

Online option: The Open University offers a fully online BSc (Hons) Cyber Security (UK qualification, NFQ-recognised via NARIC) with flexible 3-6 year pacing.

Lowest CAO points: TUS Thurles (US819) at 234 points is the most accessible full-time Level 8 programme, followed by TUS Limerick (US827) at 244. Both include industry placements.

Tip for Leaving Cert students: Programmes with mandatory industry placements (UL 30-week co-op, TUS Thurles 6-month paid, MTU Cork) significantly improve graduate employability. A 30-week placement is effectively a 7-month job interview.

Notable gap: TCD and Maynooth have no dedicated cybersecurity programme at any level. TCD, Ireland's highest-ranked university, offers security only as modules within broader CS strands.

Ireland's largest cluster of cybersecurity provision sits at postgraduate level, with 26+ MSc programmes, 3 HDips, 15 PGDips, and 25+ micro-credentials. Many are Springboard+ funded (free if unemployed, 10% fee if employed). MTU Cork is the national centre of gravity: it hosts CyberSkills.ie, Cyber Ireland, and offers the widest range from micro-credentials to PhD.

Ireland's biggest structural tailwind in cybersecurity history

Ireland is expanding from ~450 regulated entities (NIS1) to 4,500-6,000 under NIS2. The NCSC's CyberFundamentals (CyFun) framework will become the national assessment scheme, with self-registration expected July 2026 and first audits mid-2027. ISO 27001 covers approximately 70% of NIS2 requirements. Fines run up to €10 million or 2% of global turnover. Candidates who combine technical certs (Security+, SC-200) with GRC credentials (ISO 27001, eventually CISM) sit at the intersection of the two largest hiring waves in Irish tech.
