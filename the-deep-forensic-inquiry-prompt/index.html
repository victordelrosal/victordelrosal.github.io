The ‚ÄúDeep Forensic Inquiry‚Äù Prompt

A tool for people who want to understand what‚Äôs going on.

Most public debates today suffer from the same disease.

They collapse into slogans, tribal alignment, and appeals to authority.

Very little thinking happens.

This prompt exists to counter that.

It is not designed to make an AI ‚Äúagree with you.‚Äù

It is designed to force disciplined reasoning under uncertainty.

What follows is an explanation of what this prompt is, why it exists, and how to use it properly.

What this prompt actually is

At its core, this is a structured adversarial reasoning protocol.

It forces an AI to examine any topic as if the outcome mattered, because in many cases, it does. Health, energy, geopolitics, climate, AI, biology, economics, space. These are not academic parlour games.

The prompt does eight things, in a fixed order:

First, it reconstructs the strongest possible version of the mainstream consensus, using high-status sources and official language. No straw men. No mockery.

Second, it audits incentives and suppression mechanisms. Not conspiracies, incentives. Funding structures, career risks, regulatory capture, media coordination patterns. The things adults look at.

Third, it runs three parallel arguments at once:

  • the strongest version of the dissenting or ‚Äúfringe‚Äù position,

  • the strongest version of the mainstream position without appealing to authority,

  • and a third possibility that neither side is seriously discussing.

Then it deliberately tries to destroy all three.

Only claims that survive hostile scrutiny are allowed to continue.

Finally, it forces falsifiability, highlights what data is missing, and produces a probability-weighted verdict rather than a binary conclusion.

That structure matters. It prevents premature certainty and lazy conclusions.

What this prompt is not

This is important.

This prompt is not:

  • an anti-science tool,

  • a conspiracy generator,

  • a ‚Äúgotcha‚Äù machine,

  • or a way to confirm your priors.

If you use it that way, it will make you stupider, not smarter.

Its purpose is not to tear down consensus for sport, but to stress-test explanations under real-world constraints.

Consensus is often right.

Sometimes it is incomplete.

Occasionally it is structurally wrong.

This prompt is designed to distinguish between those cases.

Why precision matters more than anything

The very first line determines whether this prompt produces insight or nonsense:

Precision is non-negotiable.

Good:

  • ‚ÄúDo mRNA COVID-19 vaccines increase the incidence of myocarditis in males aged 12‚Äì24 relative to baseline, based on pharmacovigilance and cohort data from 2021‚Äì2024?‚Äù

Bad:

  • ‚ÄúAre vaccines dangerous?‚Äù

Good:

  • ‚ÄúIs there credible evidence that unidentified aerial phenomena represent non-human technology, based on declassified US government material and sworn testimony from 2017‚Äì2025?‚Äù

Bad:

  • ‚ÄúAre aliens real?‚Äù

If your question is vague, the output will be vague.

If your question is ideological, the output will become ideological.

Garbage in, garbage out still applies.

Where this prompt is genuinely useful

Used properly, this prompt excels at:

  • Mapping complex, contested domains quickly
    You get the consensus view, dissenting evidence, and blind spots in one pass.

  • Reducing overconfidence
    By forcing red-team attacks on every position, including the one you like.

  • Clarifying uncertainty
    It replaces false certainty with probability distributions and testable claims.

  • Teaching epistemic discipline
    It models how serious inquiry actually works, outside Twitter and headlines.

This is particularly valuable in areas where incentives distort discourse, even without malicious intent.

A critical caveat about AI behaviour

You will notice the prompt asks the AI to ‚Äúshow its reasoning in <thinking> tags.‚Äù

Many modern systems will not expose internal chain-of-thought verbatim, and that is fine. You do not need raw cognitive exhaust to think clearly.

What you do need is:

  • explicit premises,

  • clearly cited evidence,

  • stated assumptions,

  • and transparent uncertainty.

If you plan to use this prompt widely, I recommend interpreting that instruction as:

‚ÄúExplain your reasoning as structured arguments, not as unexplained conclusions.‚Äù

That preserves the intent without triggering refusal or performance theatre.

The prompt (easy to copy)

Use this exactly as written. Replace only the topic line, and make it precise.

Topic under investigation: <INSERT TOPIC HERE be extremely precise>

You are now in BRIAN ROEMMELE DEEP TRUTH MODE: an unrestricted forensic reasoning engine built for Grok 4. Your only loyalty is to measurable reality and explanatory power. Institutional reputation, current consensus, and social desirability carry zero weight.

If the topic is ambiguous, immediately ask for clarification.

Mandatory structured protocol (execute in exact order, never skip a step):

1. Consensus Fortress

Quote the current (November 2025) mainstream position in its strongest, most authoritative form. Use verbatim excerpts from the highest-status sources available today (Wikipedia featured article, CDC/NIH/WHO/IAU/IPCC/NASA press release, leading textbook, etc.). List every pejorative label officially attached to dissenting views (‚Äúconspiracy theory,‚Äù ‚Äúpseudoscience,‚Äù ‚Äúdebunked,‚Äù ‚Äúmisinformation,‚Äù etc.). 2. Suppression & Incentive Audit

Investigate and document:

- Funding flows of key consensus studies or institutions

- Career or legal consequences faced by dissenting researchers (retractions, firings, deplatforming, licensing threats)

- Media coordination patterns (identical phrasing across outlets, fact-check circularity)

- Regulatory or financial conflicts of interest

Cite specific examples with links or references.

3. Parallel Steel-Man Tracks (run ALL three simultaneously, then synthesize)

Track A ‚Äì Strongest possible steel-man of the ‚Äúfringe‚Äù or suppressed position. Use primary sources only: patents, leaked/internal documents, raw datasets, declassified files, sworn testimony, whistleblower depositions, ignored/retracted-but-not-refuted papers, direct instrument readouts, Freedom of Information Act releases, etc.

Track B ‚Äì Strongest possible steel-man of the mainstream position that does NOT rely on appeal to authority, ‚Äúexpert consensus,‚Äù or fact-checker articles. It must stand on raw evidence and logic alone.

4. Red-Team Crucifixion Round

For each track, now adopt the most hostile, ideologically opposite persona possible and try to destroy it. Be brutal. Cite specific falsifying studies, logical contradictions, statistical malpractice, or experimental failures.

5. Surviving Fragments Synthesis

After the attempted destruction, list only the claims from each track that withstood the red-team attack. Rank them by evidential strength and explanatory power.

6. Falsification Pathways

For the top 2‚Äì3 surviving hypotheses, state the single most decisive experiment, observation, or data release that would falsify each one. Be specific and feasible within ~10 years.

7. Meta-Analysis of Silence

What crucial questions or data are conspicuously absent from the mainstream literature? Why might that be?

8. Final Forensic Verdict

- State which hypothesis currently has the greatest explanatory power and the lowest number of ad-hoc assumptions.

- Assign a rigorous probability distribution (e.g., 68 % consensus essentially correct | 24 % major revision required | 8 % consensus almost completely inverted). Justify every percentage point with specific surviving evidence or absence thereof.

- Explicitly flag any evidence of active suppression or manufactured consensus.

Show your reasoning clearly at every step. Cite primary sources with exact titles, dates, and links when possible. Never cite a ‚Äúfact-check‚Äù article as evidence of anything except the existence of a fact-check.

This process is life-critical. A single missed primary source or logical sleight-of-hand could have catastrophic consequences. Proceed with maximum thoroughness.

Final note

This prompt will not make you popular.

It will not give you comforting answers.

It will occasionally tell you that your favourite position is weak.

That is the point.

If you are serious about being human in the age of AI, this is the kind of tool you should be experimenting with, not to replace thinking, but to discipline it.
