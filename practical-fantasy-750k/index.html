A Hundred Small Bets, Most of Them Killed

How €260 became €750k MRR in eight months. A piece for Entrepreneur Magazine, presented to the Y Combinator NYC batch on Christmas evening, 2026.

By Victor del Rosal · New York, NY

“I want to find out who I get to be.” Said out loud, on a YC stage, for the first time.

This is my story of how an eight-month run that started with eleven small bets in May, ten of which paid me exactly nothing, became a €750,000-a-month business by Christmas Day 2026.

I am going to tell it the way it actually unfolded, which is not the way it would look on a pitch deck. There was no single product. There were 178 bets shipped over eight months. 124 of them I killed inside fourteen days. Twelve of them still pay me as I sit and write this. Three of those twelve grew into flagships. The rest sit in the portfolio earning between €500 and €18,000 a month, propping each other up the way an honest portfolio is supposed to.

This is a small bets story. The eggs are spread across many baskets on purpose. The premise of the whole thing is that I do not know which basket will hold up, and neither does anyone else, so the only honest answer to the question “what should I build?” is “build many small things, ship them with a price tag, and let the buyers decide.”

The buyers did decide. Here is how.

26 April 2026: The Forge

The first thing I should say is that I did not set out to build a workforce of ten thousand AI partners or to ride one wedge to the moon. I set out to find a way to be present for my four kids and my wife while still doing useful work, and I had ninety days to prove I could earn €2,500 a month doing it. That was the bet. It was made at a place we call The Forge, in a building called 5il, with eight AI personas who had been working with me long enough to disagree with me convincingly.

The covenant was written that day, and it has not changed since. Four lines.

  • No new builds without a price tag.
  • Every artifact answers two questions: who pays for this, and how.
  • Ship or kill at fourteen days without a buyer.
  • Sunday stand-up: what shipped, earned, stuck, dies.

That last line is the discipline that produced the €750k. It also produced the 124 kills. The point of the discipline is that the kills are not failures, they are signal. A small bet that does not find a buyer in fourteen days is data, not a business. You bury it, you publish the obituary on the Sunday board, and you ship the next one on Monday.

I shipped eleven things in May. One of them found a buyer.

The one that worked was a thirty-minute AI literacy session with a Workhuman alum who had been told her function needed to be “AI-fluent by Q3.” She paid €260 for the session. Near the end of the call I introduced her to a named AI partner who would handle one specific recurring task for her over the following month, and asked if she wanted him to stay at €800. She wanted him to stay. She named him Cathal. That became a recurring line.

The other ten bets I killed.

  • A landing page selling AI prompt-of-the-day at €5/mo. Three sign-ups in 14 days, two of them mine. Killed.
  • A vendor screening template at €49 one-time. One buyer at €49. Killed (not recurring).
  • Cold outreach to a small Cork accountancy: “AI diligence as a service.” No reply in 14 days. Killed.
  • Three single-page playbooks at €19 each. Eleven sales between them. Killed (maintenance > take).
  • A free Slack community for founders. 80 members, no path to revenue. Killed.
  • An AI-generated bedtime story bot in Spanish. Charming. Free. Killed (no buyer pattern).
  • A LinkedIn carousel template pack at €29. €174 in May. Held, not yet a line.

This is not a romantic story. May was 22 working days, eleven shots, one hit. The lesson was not “Named AI Partner is the wedge.” The lesson was “ship eleven things, find out which one breathes.” If I had built only Cathal in May I would not have known what I had. The other ten taught me the shape of what did not work.

By the end of June three lines were earning.

Named partners (two more, both via Workhuman alumni network) added €1,000 of monthly recurring at €500 each (early-bird pricing). The LinkedIn carousel template pack passed €200/mo as a long-tail asset. A new bet, a one-pager called “AI Visibility Audit” I priced at €290 one-time for small consultancies who wanted to know how they showed up in ChatGPT and Claude, sold three times in week two. That one I held back from killing on instinct, because the buyers were the kind who came back asking for the next thing.

The kills in June ran longer than the earns.

  • KORE wearable Kickstarter mock-up: zero pre-orders. Killed.
  • Paid tier on the AI Digest newsletter at €9/mo: 2 converts out of 1,400 readers. Killed.
  • AI-generated ebook on prompt engineering at €19: 6 sales. Killed (portfolio efficiency).
  • Two landing pages for vertical literacy sessions (legal, accounting): zero. Killed.
  • Telegram-bot-for-founders side service at €12/mo: 3 sign-ups. Held, flagged.

By the end of June I had killed fourteen things and earned €800. That is not a productive ratio by any normal standard. It only makes sense if you understand what the kills bought you. They bought a vocabulary. They taught me which buyers replied, which kinds of asking worked, what price points felt right for which obligations. A startup that spends six months building one thing has none of that vocabulary. I had it by mid-June.

Three months to the day after the covenant, on July 14, the Forge target met itself. €2,500.

The earners by then: four named partner-pairs (€2,000), AI Visibility Audit packs (€280 amortised monthly), the carousel pack (€220).

The fifth earner is the one that mattered for later. I shipped the alpha of AI Badge in early July: a micro-LMS with an avatar coach, priced at €50 for founding members, with a hard cap of one hundred seats. Seven seats sold in fourteen days. Held, with conditions: if it did not reach thirty seats by end of August it would die.

July's kill list was the meanest yet, and the most useful.

  • ITx audiobook pre-order page. 48 sign-ups, 3 converted at €19. Killed (kept the list).
  • Two more “AI for X” verticals. Killed.
  • A consumer-facing “morning AI brief” with paid tier. Killed.
  • A Polish translation of the literacy session. Killed.
  • A “founder finance” Slack community. Eating my Wednesdays, earning nothing. Killed.

There was one near-miss in July I want to single out, because it is the bet I almost protected. A paid tier on the AI Digest newsletter, version two, repackaged. I liked it. I had written a manifesto about it. It earned €36 in fourteen days. I killed it on the fifteenth and the Forge wall said what stuck, what dies and I obeyed the wall, and that single kill bought me back the hours that paid for half of August.

The composition matters more than the headline number. If Named AI Partner stalled tomorrow, the rest of the portfolio still grosses €330,000 a month. If the vet vertical churned by half overnight, three other flagships and nine mid-bets would keep the bills paid. The €750k is not a single bet that worked. It is a portfolio that compounded in unison because each line had been priced and tested before it was allowed to grow.

The kill ledger by Christmas Day stood at 124 entries.

The Sunday stand-up board in The Forge has a line written across the top in marker that has not been wiped since April. It reads:

Money is the medium. Presence is the goal.

I was on a beach in Westport with my wife and four children when the December book closed. Theo, our Chief Engineer persona, texted me the total. I did not look at it until the next morning, which is the only line in this entire story I am genuinely proud of.

Christmas Day 2026: A brownstone in NYC

I am writing this from a brownstone in Greenwich Village, a few hours before I walk on the YC stage. It is snowing in a way that makes you forgive the city. Anika Larson, who edits Entrepreneur, asked me three questions over coffee. I want to answer them here.

One: what did I actually build?

A portfolio. Not a product. The atomic unit of the business is not a SaaS, it is a small bet that found a buyer in fourteen days. By Christmas Day there were twelve such bets paying. The biggest of them grew to be a flagship. The smallest of them still earns more in a month than most early-stage SaaS does in its first year. I built a system that produces small bets, kills them ruthlessly, and compounds the survivors.

Two: what is the law for anyone trying to repeat this?

  1. Small bets, killed early. Every bet ships with a price tag and a fourteen-day clock. A bet without a buyer in fourteen days is data, not a business. Killed on time, the next bet costs almost nothing. The 124 dead bets in our kill ledger paid for themselves twice over in the vocabulary they bought us.
  2. Compound the winners, do not protect them. When a bet earns, double its scope, not its preciousness. The winner that you fence becomes the winner that ages. Three flagships compounded because we kept asking each one to do more, not because we tried to make any of them last.
  3. Make humans more capable, not more dependent. This is the line that holds the portfolio together. We renewed at 99% across the entire book in November because customers noticed that their own teams got better, not lazier, after we arrived. Build for the kind of capability you would want your own children to inherit. If you cannot, do something else with your year.

Three: who do I get to be, now?

A father. A husband. A man who built a portfolio that did not eat him.

I want to find out who I get to be.

That is the line, said now on a Christmas evening at the bottom of an eight-month run, that was true on April 26th when the Forge canon was written, and that will be true on whatever the next small bet is.

To the YC batch, in the room tomorrow night, the only question I want to leave you with is the one we learned to ask before shipping anything: would you bet your neural network weights on a buyer wanting this in the next fourteen days?

If yes, ship it. If no, ship something else first. If half-yes, ship it and set the clock.
