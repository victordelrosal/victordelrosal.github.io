Twenty-Four Bets, Six That Stuck

How €260 became €750k MRR in eight months. A piece for Entrepreneur Magazine, presented to the Y Combinator NYC batch on Christmas evening, 2026.

By Victor del Rosal · New York, NY

“I want to find out who I get to be.” Said out loud, on a YC stage, for the first time.

This is the story of how a portfolio of twenty-four small bets, six of which stuck, became a €750,000-a-month business in eight months.

I am telling it the way it actually unfolded, which is not the way it would look on a pitch deck. There was no single product. There were twenty-four bets I tried or seriously considered between May and December 2026. Six of them found a paying floor and stayed. Eight more taught us something useful before we shelved them. The remaining ten taught us nothing we did not already suspect, and were closed out without ceremony. The batting average was roughly one in four, which is what an honest portfolio looks like when you let buyers decide.

The eggs were spread across many baskets on purpose. The premise was that I did not know which basket would hold up. Most days I still do not. What I had instead was a discipline for shipping small, pricing early, and reading the signal coming back, plus a body of work in the SmallBets folder that I had been quietly building since long before April. By May the folder already held nineteen course landing pages, a published book, a college credential, a paid client engagement at Portwest, a daily AI newsletter, the Claude Code Beginners Guide, and the alpha of a micro-LMS called AI Badge. The work that paid off in 2026 was not new work. It was old work that finally found its buyer.

Here is how the year actually went.

26 April 2026: The Forge

On the day this began I was not trying to build a workforce of ten thousand AI partners or to ride one wedge to the moon. I was trying to find a way to be present for my four kids and my wife while still doing useful work, and I had ninety days to prove I could earn €2,500 a month doing it. That was the bet. It was made at a place we call The Forge, in a building called 5il, with eight AI personas who had been working with me long enough to disagree with me convincingly.

The covenant was written that day. No new builds without a price tag. Every artifact answers two questions: who pays for this, and how. Ship small, price early, read the signal. Sunday stand-up: what shipped, what earned, what stuck, what dies. The point of the discipline is that the misses are not failures. They are the field that makes the hit possible.

I started in May by reaching back into the SmallBets folder and pulling out the things that already had some form. Nine bets shipped between May and mid-July. Four of them earned.

The first hit was AI Badge Avatar Coach. I had been quietly running the micro-LMS for a small cohort of NCI students since the spring, and at our 10K-Strong feasibility ranking in early May, AI Badge had scored highest of seven wedges. The Avatar Coach pilot launched mid-May at €19 a month per seat with a hard cap of one hundred founding members. Seven seats sold in the first fortnight. Forty by the end of June. By mid-July it crossed two hundred paying subscribers. It became the flagship I had not expected to ship first.

The second hit was the Stripe checkout layer. Theo built it in three days. Three live links by the second Sunday stand-up: AI Digest, the Beginners Guide, and the course revival page. Stripe is not a product. It is infrastructure, but it unlocked five of the lines that followed. Without it the other hits would have been free.

The third hit was the course revival. I had nineteen landing pages live on the helios and HUMANLIKE micro-degree surfaces, dormant since 2024. I picked one (helios), rebuilt the offer at €49 a month, and pointed three weeks of LinkedIn warm reach at it. Fifty paying subscribers by mid-July. €2,450 MRR from one revived course alone. The Forge target was met three days later.

The fourth hit was vis-audit recurring monitoring. A €290 one-pager I had run earlier as a one-shot AI Visibility Audit for small consultancies. In late June, on Sophie's nudge, I pivoted it from one-shot to €49 a month weekly diff email. Combilift's existing demo footage carried the messaging. Five retainers by mid-July. It would compound through the autumn.

The other five bets in the spring window did not earn. AI Digest paid tier at €8/month converted two readers out of fourteen hundred and was closed at day fourteen. AVEx, the WhatsApp AI health companion, hit an authentication and billing wall I had underestimated by six to eight weeks. Author/Thought-Leader Voice Agent, the 5/10 wedge from the 10K-Strong ranking, failed on tone fidelity in the first three pilots. Reconocimiento as a lead magnet returned three meetings and zero conversions in fourteen days. And the AI Steering Committee productisation pilot, run with one early SME for €750 a month in late June, technically worked but I am counting it inside the second wave, because the revenue did not hit the books until July.

That fifth bet, the Steering Committee, is the one that began as a long-tail and ended the year as a real line. I had been quietly running an AI Steering Committee for Portwest since April. In late June we packaged the engagement shape into an SME retainer at €750 a month with a quarterly Victor-fronted review. Three signed in July. It earned us its first hit by the start of August.

Between mid-July and the end of September I shipped six more bets. Two of them earned.

The fifth hit was CAIO-on-Demand, productised under the CAIO Codex Firm Licence. The CAIO content I had been building for two years finally found its commercial form: a monthly board note, a passive AI posture monitor for the firm, a quarterly readiness review. Three pilot firms in early August at €249/month. Nine by mid-September. Seventeen by the end of October. It would grow into a flagship by November, but the second hit of the summer set the foundation.

The sixth hit was the Managed AI Agent Business in dental. This is the headline bet from the €1M-12mo workspace, executed. The dental vertical was the first one we tested. Independent practices in Ireland: fragmented, process-standardised, suffering predictable pain in reception, recall, and no-show management. I priced the engagement at €5,000 per month per practice as a managed front-desk and back-office AI service. The first signed contract closed on August 27 after a four-week pilot. By the end of September we had four practices live. By the end of November, seventy-three. By Christmas it was the largest single revenue line in the entire portfolio.

The four bets that did not earn in this window were not failures of effort. They were honest reads of timing. ORBIT, the executive relationship intelligence wedge from the MRR-2500 ranking, was premature; it needed a real product, not a positioning. The Solopreneur AE-Builder, the 6/10 flagship wedge from 10K-Strong, was right on the model but wrong on the time horizon; we parked it per our own verdict. Sol, the multi-LLM memory SaaS, was the red-ocean call; we shipped a power-user tier for two weeks and closed it. Compliance Sentinel, the EU AI Act Article 4 monitoring agent, was the partner-channel call; it belonged inside Joe Wilde's Agentive distribution, and trying to run it alongside CAIO-on-Demand confused the buyer.

The composition of the six hits at the end of September had a shape that mattered. One large bet was just becoming visible (Managed AI Agent), one was compounding fast (AI Badge), one was a slow steady earner (vis-audit), one was a productised retainer line (Steering Committee), one was a high-touch firm licence (CAIO-on-Demand), and one was a revived back-catalogue product (helios). Different price points, different obligations, different buyers, different sales motions. No single point of failure.

Between October and the end of November I shipped six more bets. None of them stuck as new lines. The growth came from the existing six.

The reason is the boring one: when a hit is compounding, the best use of next week is feeding it, not testing a new bet. AI Badge passed fourteen hundred subscribers in October and signed its first enterprise pilot, a UK driving school chain, at €8,000 per month. The Managed AI Agent Business broke open in November when the vet vertical fell into our lap via a Workhuman alum's husband: seventy-three clinics signed in six weeks, each typically buying two pair-engagements at €1,250 per month. CAIO-on-Demand passed forty firms by mid-November, and three of them upgraded to a retainer tier at €1,500 per month. vis-audit moved into its first three enterprise audits at €5,000 each. The Steering Committee added five SMEs in October alone.

The bets we shipped alongside did not take. The Beginners Guide paid tier drew some buyers but did not compound and was held as a passive asset. The Cohort Tutor Marketplace was premature exactly as our verdict had said it would be. The Coco y Mango paid kids-app pivot found its first buyers at €4.99 a month but the consumer pricing window did not match the storytelling motion. The ITx Codex pre-order monetisation, version two, returned zero conversions out of two thousand one hundred readers; the audience wanted Codex content, not a hardback. KORE, the chest-levelling PE wearable, came up twice in the autumn and was shelved twice. And the third one I want to name was mine: the Sunday Bulletin for SME founders, a €39 a month executive briefing I drafted in late October on the theory that the Portwest Steering Committee shape could be syndicated. It hit €1,200 of MRR in three weeks and plateaued. We folded its best subscribers into the Steering Committee product and quietly retired the standalone bulletin.

The pattern of the autumn is the one nobody tells you about. Most of the growth comes from the bets you already won. The new bets you ship in the breakout months teach you what you already suspected. Their job is to keep the portfolio honest, not to find the next flagship. The next flagship usually came two quarters ago and you did not know it yet.

Three more bets shipped in December. None of them earned as new lines. The driving-school expansion of the Managed AI Agent Business pulled the trajectory clear of €750,000 on its own, with thirty-one new schools onboarded between December 8 and Christmas Eve. design-md-forge as a recurring subscription found fifty-one firms at €39 per month but stayed long-tail. The HUMANLIKE micro-degree relaunch, considered in early December, was shelved before shipping; the audience was too niche for the price point, and Sophie and I disagreed honestly about it for two Sundays before agreeing to defer it to Q2 2027.

Six lines were paying. The December book looked like this.

The remaining €750k is rounded out by a long tail of small lines totalling around 1% of revenue.

The composition matters more than the headline. If the Managed AI Agent Business stalled tomorrow, the rest of the portfolio still grosses €330,000 a month. If the vet vertical churned by half overnight, three flagships and three mid-bets would still keep the bills paid. Power-law distributions tell you that one bet will dominate and you should resource it. They do not tell you to put all your eggs in that basket before you know which one it is.

The Sunday stand-up board in The Forge has a line written across the top in marker that has not been wiped since April. It reads:

Money is the medium. Presence is the goal.

I was on a beach in Westport with my wife and four children when the December book closed. Theo, our Chief Engineer persona, texted me the total. I did not look at it until the next morning, which is the only line in this entire story I am genuinely proud of.

Christmas Day 2026: A brownstone in NYC

I am writing the closing paragraphs of this from a brownstone in Greenwich Village, a few hours before I walk on the YC stage. It is snowing in a way that makes you forgive the city. Anika Larson, who edits Entrepreneur, asked me three questions over coffee. I want to answer them here.

One: what did I actually build?

A portfolio with a roughly one-in-four batting average. Twenty-four bets tried or seriously considered, six of which stuck and stayed. The biggest of the six grew to dominate the revenue mix at 56% by December. The five smaller hits kept the portfolio honest and the business diversified. The eighteen that did not stick taught us the vocabulary that produced the six. I did not build a product. I built a system that produces small bets, prices them early, reads the signal, and resources whichever bet returns the signal first.

Two: what is the law for anyone trying to repeat this?

  1. The batting average is roughly one in four. Plan the portfolio for it. Six hits out of twenty-four was not extraordinary. It was the floor of what an honest portfolio looks like. The eighteen that did not hit were not waste; they were the field of vocabulary that made the six possible. If you cannot tolerate sixty-five to seventy-five percent of your work not converting, do not run a portfolio. Find one bet you can stake the year on and stake it.
  2. Concentrate where it compounds. Among the six hits, one grew to ten times the size of the next biggest. That is what portfolios do. When the signal arrives, you resource the bet that is sending it, not the bet you wish was. The vet vertical was a chance referral. We chose to feed it instead of the bet we had been protecting.
  3. Make humans more capable, not more dependent. This is the line that holds the portfolio together. We renewed at 99% across the entire book in November because customers noticed that their own teams got better, not lazier, after we arrived. Build for the kind of capability you would want your own children to inherit. If you cannot, do something else with your year.

Three: who do I get to be, now?

A father. A husband. A man who built a portfolio that did not eat him.

I want to find out who I get to be.

That is the line, said now on a Christmas evening at the bottom of an eight-month run, that was true on April 26th when the Forge canon was written, and that will be true on whatever the next bet is.

To the YC batch, in the room tomorrow night, the only question I want to leave you with is the one we learned to ask before shipping anything: would you bet your neural network weights on a buyer being there in the next fourteen days?

If yes, ship it. If no, ship something else first. If half-yes, ship it and watch the signal.

Compound math is unforgiving in both directions. It built this. It can erase it. Which is why the line written in marker across the top of the Sunday stand-up board has not changed since April 26, and which is the only line I want you to take with you off the stage:
