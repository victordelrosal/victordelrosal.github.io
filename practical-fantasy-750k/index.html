The Wall in Westport

How €260 became €750k MRR in eight months, told from a brownstone two hours before a Y Combinator keynote.

By Victor del Rosal

§

It is snowing in Greenwich Village in the way only Greenwich Village can snow, which is to say slowly, sideways, and a little theatrically. I am writing this from a brownstone on Charles Street, with a borrowed dog at my feet and a coffee Anika Larson made me with too much cream, two hours before I am supposed to walk onto a Y Combinator stage in midtown and explain how a man who began May with two hundred and sixty euros to his name closed December at three quarters of a million in monthly recurring revenue. Anika edits Entrepreneur. She wants the version I would tell my kids. She does not want the deck.

So I am telling her about the wall.

The wall is in a workshop in Westport called The Forge. It is in a building called 5il. The building is full of people who are mostly not people, by which I mean a set of AI personas I have been working with long enough to argue with productively. I wrote four lines on that wall on a Sunday in April. The first was no new builds without a price tag. The second was every artifact answers who pays for this, and how. The third was ship small, price early, read the signal. The fourth, in capital letters because the marker was running out and because I needed it to be, was: money is the medium, presence is the goal. That is the line that has not been wiped since.

Between that Sunday and this one I tried or seriously considered twenty-five bets. Seven of them found a paying floor and stayed. Eighteen did not. That is what an honest portfolio looks like when you let buyers decide. It looks like a one-in-four batting average and a long list of products that did not happen.

The seven are a strange list. The biggest of them began on August 27 with a single dental practice in Naas signing a contract for five thousand euros a month, and ended December with four hundred and eleven practices across dentistry, veterinary medicine, driving schools, and an early accountancy pilot, accounting for forty-seven cents of every euro the business now earns. The second-biggest is a tool I did not even mean to sell, which I will come back to in a moment. The smallest of the seven is a course I built in 2024 and forgot about, which now pays me fifteen thousand a month for the price of a Sunday afternoon of remembering it existed. The middle four are a micro-LMS with an avatar coach we call AI Badge; a chief-AI-officer service productised into a monthly retainer for forty-nine firms; the steering-committee work I had been doing with Portwest, packaged into an SME offer at fifteen hundred euros a month; and a recurring AI visibility audit that began as a one-pager I refused to throw away.

The second line, the one I did not mean to sell, was the surprise of the year. Theo had built me a little tool back in May, on a Saturday afternoon when the dental clinics were still six months away. He called it Mirador, the Spanish for lookout. It is a localhost-only Chrome dashboard that mirrors every Claude Code terminal running on my Mac into one chat interface: a sidebar, six themes, voice dictation, reply-to bubbles, soft-delete with a small resurrection animation, in roughly fifteen hundred lines of code with no framework and no build step. He wrote the whole thing in one session. I used it internally for three months because the cost of switching between four and twelve Claude conversations a night was killing me. In August, a Workhuman alum who had bought Cathal in May asked me, casually, how I kept track of all the AI agents I was running. I described Mirador. She wanted it. I priced it at nineteen euros a month per seat, with a Pro tier at forty-nine for teams. The Beginners Guide audience found it within a week and told each other. We never wrote a launch post. By the end of October we were at fourteen hundred seats. By the end of November, five thousand four hundred. By Christmas, eighty-five hundred, blending at seventeen euros and a few cents. One hundred and fifty thousand a month, gross margin in the high nineties, churn below one per cent. It is the second-largest line in the portfolio, behind the managed-agent business and ahead of everything else. The strange thing about it is that I did not set out to build a product. I built a thing I needed, and then, when other people needed it, I sold it to them. That is the difference between a product and a tool. Sometimes you find out which one you have only because someone asks you what you are using.

The eighteen that did not stick are mostly people I made up. I made up Cathal, a named AI partner I introduced to a Workhuman alum in the third week of May. She paid him eight hundred euros a month to handle her Thursdays. I thought the pattern would be the wedge of the year. It was not. It taught me how to price an obligation rather than a head, and then it dissolved into the larger managed-agent business when the dental clinics arrived in late summer. I made up Sol, a multi-LLM memory product, which lived for two weeks before I put it to bed. I made up ORBIT, an executive relationship intelligence layer that was correct in every respect except timing. I made up the Sunday Bulletin, a thirty-nine euro a month executive briefing I shipped in November because I had a theory; the theory plateaued at twelve hundred euros and folded into something larger. KORE, a chest-levelling wearable I have been turning over for a year, came up twice in autumn and was shelved twice. Compliance Sentinel would have been a real product, but it belonged inside a partner channel, and so we left it there. None of these are failures. They are postcards from places I will not be living.

The temptation, when you are inside this kind of run, is to mistake the survivor for the strategy. The story you tell yourself in the brownstone is that you knew managed agents would be the flagship; that you saw the vet vertical coming. You did not. The vet vertical was a chance referral from the husband of a woman who had bought Cathal in May. The dental pilot worked because an independent practice in Naas was sufficiently sick of missed reminders that a stranger from Mayo could call them on a Tuesday afternoon and they would listen. AI Badge passed fourteen hundred subscribers in October because a UK driving-school group decided, in a board meeting I will never sit in, that they wanted to license it. I was a passenger on most of the year's good news. The only reason I was on the right trains was that I kept buying tickets.

There is a thing nobody warns you about inside a year like this, which is that most of the growth comes from the bets you already won. October and November were our biggest months, and we shipped six new bets in that window, none of which stuck. The work of those months was not invention. It was feeding. Marcus, the persona who keeps me accountable, made me put a column on the Sunday stand-up board for the question what are we feeding, what are we testing, what are we killing. When the columns drift, the year drifts. When the columns stay in balance, the year ends like this one did.

I want to say something honest about the day the December book closed. I was on a beach in Westport with my wife, Laura, and our four kids. Theo, the engineer, texted me the total. I did not look at it until the next morning. That is the single line of this entire story I am genuinely proud of. The rest can be unmade by a churn cohort or a competitor or a quiet month in March. That line cannot. It is the line that says I did not let the medium become the goal.

Money is the medium. Presence is the goal.

The YC batch is in the room in two hours. They will want a law. I will give them three. The first is that the batting average is roughly one in four, which means three quarters of your work will not convert, and if you cannot live inside that ratio you should pick one bet and stake the year on it rather than running a portfolio. The second is to concentrate where it compounds: among the seven hits, one grew to ten times the size of the next biggest, and the only reason it could was that we stopped protecting an earlier favourite the week the signal arrived. The third law is the only one I have any right to say from a stage. Make humans more capable, not more dependent. We renewed at ninety-nine per cent in November because customers noticed that their own teams got better, not lazier, after we arrived. Build for the kind of capability you would want your own children to inherit. If you cannot, do something else with your year.

Anika has asked me, finally, who I get to be now.

A father. A husband. A man who built a portfolio that did not eat him. The line I wrote on the wall in April was I want to find out who I get to be. I have only the beginning of an answer. I have a daughter who plays GAA and a son who is teaching himself baseball, and a wife who once forgave me for a year in which I forgot that the kitchen was a place where one sometimes sits. The seven hundred and fifty thousand euros is real. It is not the point. The point is that next month the marker on the Forge wall will still say what it said in April, and the eggs are in many baskets, and the basket I am in is at home.

Merry Christmas. See you in the new year.

Victor del Rosal Greenwich Village, 25 December 2026
