Amygdalin and Cancer

Four decades of evidence weigh against "Vitamin B17"

Based on clinical trials, Cochrane reviews, and peer-reviewed research through 2026

The verdict: Amygdalin (laetrile) has no demonstrated anticancer efficacy in humans, and its use carries serious risk of cyanide poisoning. The Cochrane systematic review concluded that the risk-benefit balance is "unambiguously negative." Every major medical authority recommends against its use.

The Clinical Record

The entire human clinical evidence for amygdalin rests on three NCI-sponsored investigations conducted between 1978 and 1982. No controlled, randomized trial has ever been performed.

1978: NCI Retrospective Review
Cases solicited from 385,000 physicians. Of 93 submitted, only 67 were evaluable. A blinded panel of 12 oncologists identified just 6 possible responses: enough ambiguity for the NCI to authorize prospective trials, but far from persuasive.
1981: Phase I Study
Moertel et al. (JAMA) enrolled 6 advanced cancer patients. IV amygdalin was largely excreted unchanged. Oral doses of 500mg produced significant blood cyanide. Two patients who ate raw almonds during therapy developed acute cyanide toxicity.
1982: Definitive Phase II Trial
Moertel et al. (NEJM): 178 patients across Mayo Clinic, UCLA, and Memorial Sloan Kettering. Only 1 of 175 evaluable patients showed a partial response lasting just 10 weeks. 54% had measurable progression by end of IV phase. 100% had progressed by 7 months. Several patients reached blood cyanide levels approaching the lethal range.
1982 to present
No human clinical trial of amygdalin or laetrile has been conducted since. A search of ClinicalTrials.gov reveals no active, recruiting, or recently completed trials.

Why "Selective Toxicity" Fails

Amygdalin is a cyanogenic diglycoside (C₂₀H₂₇NO₁₁). Its hydrolysis proceeds in three enzymatic steps, ultimately releasing hydrogen cyanide. One gram liberates approximately 59mg of HCN: a quantity that can approach lethal thresholds.

The Krebs Theory (Disproven)

Ernst T. Krebs Jr. proposed in the 1950s that cancer cells have higher β-glucosidase (releasing cyanide at tumors) and normal cells have higher rhodanese (detoxifying cyanide). This would create a "biochemical trap."

Both premises have been experimentally refuted. Newmark et al. (1981, PNAS) found no detectable amygdalin-hydrolyzing activity in any cancer tissue examined, while the small intestinal mucosa showed high activity. Greenberg's 1980 review demonstrated that β-glucosidase levels in tumors are lower than in normal liver and intestine.

What Actually Happens

The primary site of cyanide generation is the gastrointestinal tract, where intestinal enzymes and gut microbiota efficiently cleave amygdalin. This is why oral amygdalin is roughly 40 times more toxic than intravenous administration.

Not a vitamin. The term "Vitamin B17" was coined by Krebs as a marketing strategy. Amygdalin is not recognized as a vitamin by any scientific or regulatory body. It serves no metabolic function, and no deficiency syndrome exists.

Documented Harm

Cyanide generated from amygdalin inhibits cytochrome c oxidase (mitochondrial Complex IV), shutting down cellular respiration and causing tissue hypoxia, lactic acidosis, seizures, coma, and death. The lethal oral dose of HCN ranges from 0.5 to 3.5 mg/kg body weight.

Risk Factors That Amplify Toxicity
  • Co-administration with high-dose vitamin C, which increases cyanide release and impairs detoxification
  • Concurrent consumption of raw almonds, bean sprouts, or crushed fruit pits (β-glucosidase-rich foods)
  • Vitamin B12 deficiency, which impairs the hydroxocobalamin detoxification pathway
  • Oral rather than IV administration, which exposes amygdalin to gut microbial enzymes

Case Reports

Documented fatalities include multiple children who died after eating apricot kernels. In 2005, a 68-year-old cancer patient suffered life-threatening cyanide poisoning requiring intubation after combining 3g amygdalin with 4,800 mg/day vitamin C. In 2017, a 73-year-old woman taking just three 500mg tablets (approximately 90mg cyanide, or 1.8 times the minimum lethal dose) developed multiorgan failure within 45 minutes.

The European Food Safety Authority set an acute reference dose of just 20 μg/kg body weight for cyanogenic glycosides, meaning an adult should consume no more than 3 small apricot kernels at a sitting.

The Cochrane Verdict

The Cochrane systematic review by Milazzo, Horneber, and Ernst (first published 2006, updated through 2015) searched CENTRAL, MEDLINE, EMBASE, AMED, CINAHL, and multiple trial registries without language restrictions for randomized or quasi-randomized trials.

Not a single study met the inclusion criteria. No RCT or quasi-RCT of amygdalin/laetrile for cancer has ever been conducted.

The review's conclusion: "The claims that laetrile or amygdalin have beneficial effects for cancer patients are not currently supported by sound clinical data. There is a considerable risk of serious adverse effects from cyanide poisoning. The risk-benefit balance of laetrile or amygdalin as a treatment for cancer is therefore unambiguously negative."

A companion review (2007) examined all clinical study types. Among 36 reports, none proved effectiveness, while 16 case reports documented adverse effects including 2 deaths from cyanide poisoning.

Recent Preclinical Research

A wave of laboratory research since 2020 has identified anticancer mechanisms operating independently of the original cyanide theory. In vitro studies demonstrate cell cycle arrest, apoptosis, mTOR-Akt pathway inhibition, and anti-metastatic effects at high concentrations.

Nanoparticle Delivery

The most active research frontier. Alginate-chitosan nanoparticles, amygdalin-folic acid nanoparticles, and ZnO-TiO₂-chitosan composites have demonstrated enhanced cytotoxicity in cell culture, aiming to concentrate amygdalin at tumor sites while minimizing systemic cyanide exposure.

Photo-Amygdalin

Published by Ben Feringa's group (Chemical Science, 2025): a synthetic analogue whose cyanide release is triggered by visible light rather than enzymatic hydrolysis. It proved 100-fold more potent than potassium cyanide in reducing cell viability upon irradiation, though it remains proof-of-concept.

The Gap Remains

These findings are real and published in legitimate journals. However, effective in vitro concentrations are pharmacologically unrealistic, far exceeding achievable human plasma levels. The gap between cell-culture cytotoxicity and clinical efficacy is vast. Crucially, no researcher has yet undertaken a new human clinical trial.

The Bottom Line

Forty-four years after the definitive Moertel trial, the evidence base for amygdalin as a cancer treatment has not materially changed at the clinical level. The proposed selective toxicity mechanism has been disproven. The only human trials showed no benefit and documented serious harm.

Modern molecular biology has revealed that amygdalin interacts with cancer-relevant pathways through mechanisms distinct from crude cyanide toxicity. Nanoparticle delivery and light-activated analogues offer theoretically plausible routes to safer administration. These are legitimate scientific explorations, but they are separated from clinical relevance by years of translational work and the clinical trials that no one has yet initiated.

Amygdalin/laetrile is not a proven cancer treatment, carries documented lethal risks (especially when taken orally or combined with vitamin C), and should not substitute for evidence-based oncologic care. The "Vitamin B17" label is a marketing artifact, not a biochemical reality.
