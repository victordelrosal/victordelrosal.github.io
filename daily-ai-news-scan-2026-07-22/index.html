TSMC to raise chip prices in 2027

Daily AI News Scan — Wednesday, July 22, 2026

Executive Summary

TSMC is set to raise chip-making prices by up to 10% in early 2027, driven by surging AI demand and higher manufacturing costs, with the company simultaneously pledging an additional $100 billion for Arizona fabs. On the regulatory front, a federal judge approved Anthropic's $1.5 billion copyright settlement with authors — the largest known in US history — while business leaders surveyed by analyst David Shapiro report significantly less AGI anxiety than the tech community projects. Together, these stories reflect a maturing AI infrastructure cycle marked by rising costs, legal accountability, and diverging risk perceptions.

Top Stories

1. TSMC to raise chip prices in 2027

TSMC plans to increase base prices for chip-manufacturing services by up to 10% starting in early 2027, citing sustained AI demand and elevated production costs. The announcement arrives alongside a pledge of an additional $100 billion investment in four new Arizona factories, on top of a prior $165 billion commitment. AMD also entered the rack-scale AI competition with the launch of Helios, its first integrated rack system targeting Nvidia's dominance, with Microsoft, Meta, OpenAI, and Oracle listed as early customers. On the Apple side, the company is accelerating its AI-focused M7 chip family, skipping the completion of the M6 lineup due to significant improvements in neural-processing capabilities, and has sent legal letters to former employees now at OpenAI over alleged trade secret violations.

Source: NVIDIA AI Blog

2. David Shapiro discusses real-world CEO perspectives on AI and AGI concerns

Analyst David Shapiro contends that business leaders and executives who work with AI daily are considerably less alarmed about AGI and existential risk than the discourse within the tech community implies, characterizing AI as simply another productivity technology. Separately, Google released three new Gemini models — 3.6 Flash, 3.5 Flash-Lite, and 3.5 Flash Cyber — targeting developers building AI agents with faster responses and lower costs, though the flagship Gemini Pro line was not updated. The Trump administration is also weighing restrictions on American businesses using Chinese AI models, a measure intended to protect domestic labs including OpenAI and Anthropic. The divergence between practitioner sentiment and public AI discourse has implications for how enterprises will pace adoption and risk planning.

Source: TechCrunch AI

3. US judge approves Anthropic's $1.5B settlement with authors for copyright infringement

A federal judge approved a $1.5 billion settlement between Anthropic and authors whose books were used to train Claude, marking the largest known copyright settlement in US history and establishing a significant legal precedent for AI training data liability. The settlement signals that foundation model developers face substantial financial exposure from content creators, potentially reshaping how future training datasets are assembled and licensed. Anthropic also launched a $50,000 API credit grant program for AI research focused on rare disease treatment and diagnosis, and announced that Claude Fable 5 will be included in Max and Team Premium plans starting July 20, with Pro and Team Standard users receiving a one-time $100 credit.

Source: Google News: LLM
