Pillar 1: Foundations of Data Visualization

Primary LO: LO1 | Indicative Content: Topic 1

This pillar addresses what data visualization is, why it matters, and the foundational concepts that underpin the entire discipline. It establishes the theoretical groundwork on which all subsequent pillars build.

1.1

What is Data Visualization

1.1.1

Definition and Purpose

  • Definition: Data visualization is the graphical representation of information and data, using visual elements such as charts, graphs, and maps to make patterns, trends, and outliers in data accessible to human cognition.
  • Core purpose: To leverage the human visual system (the most powerful pattern-detection mechanism we possess) to communicate quantitative and qualitative information clearly, accurately, and efficiently.
  • Distinction from information graphics: Data visualization is driven by data and aims for accuracy and insight; infographics may prioritise aesthetics or narrative and are not always data-driven.
  • The dual role: Visualization serves both exploratory purposes (the analyst discovering patterns) and explanatory purposes (communicating findings to others). Kirk (2019) refers to this as the spectrum from analysis to communication.
Reading: Kirk (2019), Ch. 1; Knaflic (2015), Ch. 1; Ward, Grinstein & Keim (2010), Ch. 1.
1.1.2

A Brief History of Data Visualization

  • Charles Joseph Minard (1869): His flow map of Napoleon's 1812 Russian campaign is often cited as the greatest statistical graphic ever drawn. It encodes six variables (army size, location, direction, temperature, latitude, longitude) in a single image, demonstrating that visualization can compress enormous complexity into comprehensible form.
  • John Snow (1854): His dot map of cholera deaths in London's Soho district identified a contaminated water pump as the disease source. A landmark example of spatial visualization driving a life-saving decision.
  • Florence Nightingale (1858): Her "coxcomb" (polar area) diagrams illustrated that more soldiers died from preventable disease than from battle wounds, persuading the British government to reform military hospital conditions. A powerful case for visualization as a tool of advocacy and persuasion.
  • William Playfair (late 18th century): Inventor of the bar chart, line chart, and pie chart. His work established the foundational chart types still in use today.
  • Why history matters: These examples demonstrate that effective visualization has always been about more than aesthetics; it is about clarity, persuasion, and consequential decision-making.

Exam Tip

Be prepared to reference specific historical examples to illustrate how visualization principles have long been used to communicate complex data and drive decisions.

Reading: Tufte (2001), Ch. 2 (Minard); Kirk (2019), Ch. 1; Ware (2012), Ch. 1.
1.1.3

The Spectrum of Visualization

  • Scientific visualization: Rendering physical phenomena (fluid dynamics, molecular structures, medical imaging). Typically three-dimensional and domain-specific.
  • Information visualization: Representing abstract, non-spatial data (financial data, organisational hierarchies, social networks). The primary focus of this module.
  • Visual analytics: The science of analytical reasoning facilitated by interactive visual interfaces. Combines automated analysis with interactive visualization to support human judgment.
Reading: Ward, Grinstein & Keim (2010), Ch. 1; Ware (2012), Ch. 1.
1.2

Data Types and Characteristics

1.2.1

Quantitative vs Categorical Data

  • Quantitative (numerical) data: Values that represent measurable quantities. Can be subjected to arithmetic operations.
    • Continuous: Can take any value within a range (temperature, revenue, time). Typically represented by line charts, scatter plots, histograms.
    • Discrete: Countable values with gaps between them (number of employees, units sold). Typically represented by bar charts.
  • Categorical (qualitative) data: Values that represent categories or groups.
    • Nominal: Unordered categories (country, product type, department). No inherent ranking.
    • Ordinal: Ordered categories where the interval between values is not necessarily equal (satisfaction ratings: low/medium/high; education level).
  • Why this matters for visualization: The data type determines which visual encodings and chart types are appropriate. Using a line chart for nominal data, for instance, implies a non-existent ordering.
Reading: Kirk (2019), Ch. 3; Ward, Grinstein & Keim (2010), Ch. 2.
1.2.2

Scales of Measurement

ScalePropertiesExamplesAppropriate Visual Encodings

OrdinalIdentity + order; unequal intervalsSatisfaction rating, education levelPosition on ordered axis, colour saturation, size

Reading: Kirk (2019), Ch. 3; Ware (2012), Ch. 5.
1.2.3

Temporal, Spatial, and Network Data

  • Temporal data: Data indexed by time. Requires attention to granularity (seconds, days, quarters), regularity (evenly vs unevenly spaced), and cyclical patterns (seasonality).
  • Spatial data: Data associated with geographic locations. Requires map projections, coordinate systems, and decisions about aggregation level (point vs area).
  • Network (relational) data: Data describing connections between entities (social networks, supply chains, organisational structures). Requires graph-based representations (nodes and edges).
  • Multivariate data: Datasets with many variables per observation. Requires dimensionality reduction or specialised techniques (parallel coordinates, small multiples).
Reading: Ward, Grinstein & Keim (2010), Ch. 2, 8-11; Kirk (2019), Ch. 3.
1.3

The Visualization Pipeline

1.3.1

From Raw Data to Visual Output

  • The pipeline: Data Acquisition > Data Filtering/Transformation > Visual Mapping > Rendering.
    • Data acquisition: Collecting data from source systems (databases, APIs, files, sensors).
    • Data filtering and transformation: Cleaning, aggregating, normalising, and structuring data for visualization. This step often consumes the majority of effort.
    • Visual mapping: The critical design step. Deciding which data attributes map to which visual channels (position, colour, size, shape). This is where design principles and perceptual science intersect.
    • Rendering: The technical process of drawing the visual representation on screen or paper.
  • Why the pipeline matters: Understanding the pipeline makes explicit that visualization is not merely "making a chart." Each stage involves decisions that can enhance or undermine the final result.
Reading: Ward, Grinstein & Keim (2010), Ch. 1, 4; Ware (2012), Ch. 1.
1.3.2

Data Preparation for Visualization

  • Data cleaning: Handling missing values, outliers, duplicates, and inconsistencies. Dirty data produces misleading visualizations regardless of design quality.
  • Aggregation decisions: Choosing the right level of granularity (daily vs monthly sales; individual transactions vs category totals). Over-aggregation hides patterns; under-aggregation creates noise.
  • Derived variables: Creating new variables from existing ones (growth rates, ratios, moving averages) that may better serve the visualization's purpose.
  • The "tidy data" principle (Hadley Wickham): Each variable forms a column, each observation forms a row, and each type of observational unit forms a table. Tidy data is the prerequisite for effective visualization.
Reading: Kirk (2019), Ch. 3; Knaflic (2015), Ch. 2.
1.4

Ethical Considerations in Data Visualization

1.4.1

Truthfulness and Integrity

  • The visualiser's responsibility: Every design decision (axis range, colour scale, chart type, annotation) shapes how the viewer interprets the data. The visualiser has an ethical obligation to represent data truthfully.
  • Tufte's Lie Factor: The ratio of the size of an effect shown in the graphic to the size of the effect in the data. A Lie Factor of 1.0 means accurate representation; values significantly above or below 1.0 indicate distortion.
  • Common distortions: Truncated y-axes that exaggerate small differences; area or volume encodings that distort proportions; cherry-picked time windows that misrepresent trends.
Reading: Tufte (2001), Ch. 2 (Graphical Integrity); Kirk (2019), Ch. 2.
1.4.2

Misleading Charts and Manipulation

  • Intentional vs unintentional misleading: Some distortions result from ignorance of design principles; others are deliberate manipulation. Both are harmful.
  • Examples of manipulation: Dual axes that create false correlations; 3D effects that distort bar heights; inverted axes (e.g., the Florida "Stand Your Ground" gun deaths chart); inconsistent scales across panels.
  • Context and framing: Showing data without appropriate context (benchmarks, historical baselines, confidence intervals) can mislead even when the chart itself is technically accurate.
Reading: Tufte (2001), Ch. 2; Kirk (2019), Ch. 2; Few (2009), Ch. 3.
1.4.3

Responsibility and Audience Consideration

  • Know your audience: The same data may need to be presented very differently to technical analysts, senior executives, and the general public. Failing to adapt can either oversimplify (losing critical nuance) or overwhelm (losing the message).
  • Data sourcing ethics: Transparency about where data comes from, how it was collected, and what limitations it carries. Visualizing biased data without acknowledging the bias perpetuates harm.
  • Accessibility as an ethical concern: Designing visualizations that exclude people with colour vision deficiencies or other disabilities is a failure of both ethics and communication.

Exam Tip

Ethics questions in the terminal exam are highly likely. Be prepared to identify ethical issues in a given visualization and propose corrections, referencing Tufte's Lie Factor and principles of graphical integrity.

Reading: Kirk (2019), Ch. 2; Tufte (2001), Ch. 2; Knaflic (2015), Ch. 1.
1.5

The Role of Visualization in Business Decision Making

1.5.1

Why Business Needs Visualization

  • Cognitive amplification: Visualization extends working memory by externalising information. A well-designed dashboard allows a decision-maker to hold and compare far more information than unaided cognition permits.
  • Speed of insight: Visual representations are processed faster than tabular data. Patterns that would take hours to discover in spreadsheets can be apprehended in seconds in a well-designed visualization.
  • Democratisation of data: Visualization makes data accessible to non-technical stakeholders, enabling broader participation in data-informed decision-making.
Reading: Ware (2012), Ch. 1; Kirk (2019), Ch. 1; Knaflic (2015), Ch. 1.
1.5.2

Visualization in the Analytics Workflow

  • Exploratory visualization: Used by analysts to understand data structure, detect anomalies, and generate hypotheses before formal modelling.
  • Explanatory visualization: Used to communicate findings, support recommendations, and persuade stakeholders. This is where Knaflic's (2015) framework (context, audience, action) is essential.
  • Monitoring and operational visualization: Dashboards and real-time displays that support ongoing operational decisions (supply chain, financial trading, customer service).
Reading: Knaflic (2015), Ch. 1-2; Kirk (2019), Ch. 1.

Pillar 2: Human Perception and Cognition

Primary LO: LO2 | Indicative Content: Topic 2

This pillar is the scientific foundation of the module. Understanding how humans see and process visual information is the prerequisite for designing visualizations that work with, rather than against, human cognition. LO2 explicitly asks for critique of advanced perception and cognition theories.

2.1

Visual Channels and Pre-attentive Attributes

2.1.1

What are Visual Channels?

  • Definition: Visual channels (also called visual variables or retinal variables) are the properties of marks (points, lines, areas) that can be used to encode data. Originally articulated by Jacques Bertin (1967), they form the vocabulary of visual encoding.
  • Primary channels: Position, length, angle, area, colour hue, colour saturation, colour luminance (brightness), shape, orientation, texture.
  • Channel effectiveness varies by data type:
    • Quantitative data: Position on a common scale is most accurate, followed by length, angle, area, and colour saturation (Cleveland & McGill, 1984).
    • Categorical data: Colour hue and shape are most effective for distinguishing unordered categories.
    • Ordinal data: Colour saturation, size, and position on an ordered axis.
Reading: Ware (2012), Ch. 5; Kirk (2019), Ch. 7; Ward, Grinstein & Keim (2010), Ch. 3.
2.1.2

Pre-attentive Processing and the Pop-out Effect

  • Pre-attentive processing: Certain visual properties are detected by the low-level visual system in under 250 milliseconds, before conscious attention is engaged. This happens in parallel across the visual field.
  • Pre-attentive attributes: Colour hue, colour intensity, size, orientation, shape, motion, spatial position, enclosure, line length, line width, curvature, added marks, flicker.
  • The pop-out effect: A single element that differs from surrounding elements on a pre-attentive attribute is detected instantly, regardless of how many distractors are present. For example, a single red dot among grey dots "pops out" immediately.
  • Conjunction search fails: If a target differs from distractors on a combination of attributes (e.g., red AND square among red circles and blue squares), pre-attentive processing does not work. Search becomes serial and slow.
  • Design implication: Use pre-attentive attributes strategically to draw attention to the most important data points. Avoid using too many pre-attentive channels simultaneously, which creates visual noise rather than clarity.

Exam Tip

Be prepared to explain what pre-attentive processing is, list several pre-attentive attributes, and discuss why conjunction searches fail. This is a core LO2 topic.

Reading: Ware (2012), Ch. 5; Kirk (2019), Ch. 7; Few (2009), Ch. 4.
2.2

Gestalt Principles

2.2.1

Overview

  • Origin: The Gestalt principles emerged from early 20th-century German psychology. "Gestalt" means "form" or "whole." The central insight is that the human visual system organises visual elements into groups and patterns, and the perceived whole is more than the sum of its parts.
  • Relevance to visualization: Gestalt principles determine how viewers group, separate, and interpret visual elements on a chart or dashboard. A designer who understands these principles can guide perception; one who does not may inadvertently create confusion.
2.2.2

The Core Gestalt Principles

PrincipleDescriptionVisualization Application
ProximityElements close together are perceived as belonging to the same groupSpacing between chart elements signals grouping (e.g., grouped bar charts)
SimilarityElements that share visual properties (colour, shape, size) are perceived as relatedUsing consistent colour for the same data series across multiple charts
EnclosureElements enclosed within a boundary are perceived as a groupUsing boxes or shaded regions to group related dashboard components
ContinuityThe eye follows smooth, continuous paths rather than abrupt changesLine charts exploit continuity; aligning chart axes creates visual flow
ConnectionElements connected by lines are perceived as related, even if spatially distantLines connecting nodes in network diagrams; line charts connecting data points
Figure-groundThe visual system separates objects (figures) from their background (ground)Ensuring data elements stand out against the chart background; strategic use of contrast
Common fateElements moving in the same direction are perceived as a groupAnimated transitions in interactive visualizations
  • Hierarchy of strength: Enclosure and connection are typically the strongest grouping cues, followed by proximity and similarity. Designers should layer these principles deliberately.

Exam Tip

The Gestalt principles are a near-certain exam topic for LO2. Be able to define each, give a visualization-specific example, and explain how misapplication can mislead viewers.

Reading: Ware (2012), Ch. 6; Kirk (2019), Ch. 7; Knaflic (2015), Ch. 3.
2.3

Contrast, Brightness, and Luminance

2.3.1

Weber's Law

  • Statement: The just-noticeable difference (JND) between two stimuli is proportional to the magnitude of the original stimulus. In visualization terms, the ability to distinguish two shades of grey depends on how dark the baseline is.
  • Design implication: Differences that are perceptible at one end of a colour scale may be imperceptible at another. Colour scales must be tested for perceptual uniformity.
Reading: Ware (2012), Ch. 4.
2.3.2

Stevens' Power Law

  • Statement: The perceived magnitude of a stimulus is a power function of its actual magnitude. Different visual attributes have different exponents: length perception is nearly linear (exponent close to 1.0), area perception is compressed (exponent around 0.7), brightness perception is also compressed.
  • Design implication: Because area perception is compressed, bubble charts and proportional symbol maps systematically underestimate differences. A circle with twice the data value should have twice the area, but viewers will perceive it as less than double.
Reading: Ware (2012), Ch. 4; Ward, Grinstein & Keim (2010), Ch. 3.
2.3.3

Simultaneous Contrast

  • Definition: The perceived brightness or colour of an element is influenced by the brightness or colour of its surroundings. A grey square appears lighter on a dark background and darker on a light background.
  • Design implication: Background choices affect how data colours are perceived. A colour that appears distinct in a design tool may become ambiguous when placed against a different dashboard background.
Reading: Ware (2012), Ch. 4.
2.4

Colour Perception and Colour Theory

2.4.1

Colour Models

  • RGB (Red, Green, Blue): Additive colour model used by screens. Useful for implementation but poor for reasoning about perceptual relationships.
  • HSL/HSV (Hue, Saturation, Lightness/Value): Separates colour into three intuitive dimensions. Hue is the "colour" (red, blue, green); saturation is the intensity; lightness is the brightness. More useful for design decisions.
  • CIELAB (L*a*b*): Perceptually uniform colour space where equal numerical distances correspond to equal perceived differences. The gold standard for colour scale design.
Reading: Ware (2012), Ch. 4; Kirk (2019), Ch. 8.
2.4.2

Colour Blindness and Accessibility

  • Prevalence: Approximately 8% of men and 0.5% of women have some form of colour vision deficiency. The most common type is red-green (deuteranopia/protanopia).
  • Design requirement: Never use colour as the sole encoding for critical information. Always provide a redundant cue (shape, label, pattern, position).
  • Testing tools: Colour Oracle, Coblis, Sim Daltonism. Designers should simulate colour-blind viewing conditions during design.
  • WCAG compliance: Web Content Accessibility Guidelines require minimum contrast ratios (4.5:1 for normal text, 3:1 for large text).
Reading: Ware (2012), Ch. 4; Kirk (2019), Ch. 8; Few (2009), Ch. 4.
2.4.3

Colour Scales for Data

Scale TypeUse CaseExample
SequentialOrdered data from low to highLight blue to dark blue for temperature
DivergingData with a meaningful midpointRed (negative) through white (zero) to blue (positive) for profit/loss
Categorical (qualitative)Unordered categoriesDistinct hues for product lines (red, blue, green, orange)
  • ColorBrewer: Cynthia Brewer's tool providing rigorously tested colour palettes for maps and visualizations. Palettes are designed for print safety, colour-blind safety, and photocopy safety.
  • Viridis: A perceptually uniform, colour-blind-friendly sequential palette widely adopted in Python (matplotlib) and R.
  • Common mistakes: Using a rainbow palette (perceptually non-uniform, confuses ordering); using too many categorical colours (limit to 6-8 distinguishable hues); using red-green encoding without accessibility fallback.
Reading: Ware (2012), Ch. 4; Kirk (2019), Ch. 8; Few (2009), Ch. 4.
2.4.4

Cultural Associations of Colour

  • Context-dependent meanings: Red may signify danger, loss, or passion in Western contexts but good fortune in Chinese culture. Green may signify profit, nature, or Islam depending on audience.
  • Design implication: When designing for international audiences, avoid relying on culturally specific colour associations. Use labels and legends to make meaning explicit.
Reading: Kirk (2019), Ch. 8.
2.5

Cognitive Load and Working Memory

2.5.1

Miller's 7 +/- 2

  • George Miller (1956): The capacity of working memory is approximately 7 (plus or minus 2) chunks of information. This is the bottleneck for human information processing.
  • Design implication: Dashboards that present more than 7 distinct categories, colour codes, or chart panels simultaneously risk overwhelming working memory. Chunking, grouping, and progressive disclosure help manage cognitive load.
Reading: Ware (2012), Ch. 7; Knaflic (2015), Ch. 3.
2.5.2

Change Blindness and Inattentional Blindness

  • Change blindness: Failure to notice significant changes in a visual scene when attention is not directed to the change. In visualization, if a dashboard updates a key metric without visual signalling (animation, colour flash), the user may not notice.
  • Inattentional blindness: Failure to notice an unexpected stimulus when attention is focused elsewhere. In visualization, critical alerts or annotations can be missed if they do not break through the viewer's current attentional focus.
  • Design implication: Use animation, colour change, or spatial repositioning to signal important changes. Do not assume that placing information on screen guarantees it will be seen.
Reading: Ware (2012), Ch. 7.
2.5.3

Cognitive Load Theory Applied to Visualization

  • Intrinsic load: The inherent complexity of the data and the task. A 20-variable dataset is intrinsically more complex than a 3-variable one.
  • Extraneous load: Unnecessary cognitive burden created by poor design: cluttered layouts, inconsistent colour coding, decorative elements that add no information.
  • Germane load: Cognitive effort directed toward understanding and integrating new information. Good design maximises germane load by reducing extraneous load.
  • The designer's goal: Minimise extraneous cognitive load so that the viewer's limited working memory is devoted to understanding the data, not deciphering the chart.
Reading: Ware (2012), Ch. 7; Knaflic (2015), Ch. 3.
2.6

Cleveland and McGill's Graphical Perception Hierarchy

2.6.1

The Ranking of Visual Encodings

RankVisual EncodingAccuracy
1 (most accurate)Position on a common scaleHighest
2Position on non-aligned scalesHigh
3LengthHigh
4Angle / SlopeModerate
5AreaLow
6VolumeLow
7Colour saturation / Colour hueLowest for quantitative
  • Practical interpretation: Bar charts (position + length) are more accurately read than pie charts (angle + area). Scatter plots (position on common scales) outperform bubble charts (which add area encoding). This hierarchy should guide chart type selection.
  • Limitations: The hierarchy applies to quantitative comparison tasks. For categorical distinction, colour hue and shape may be more appropriate than position.

Exam Tip

The Cleveland and McGill hierarchy is one of the most frequently tested frameworks in data visualization. Be prepared to cite it, explain the ranking, and apply it to justify chart type recommendations.

Reading: Ware (2012), Ch. 5; Kirk (2019), Ch. 7; Ward, Grinstein & Keim (2010), Ch. 3.
2.6.2

Implications for Chart Selection

  • Bar charts over pie charts: For comparing quantities across categories, bar charts (position encoding) are almost always superior to pie charts (angle/area encoding). The only defensible use of pie charts is when there are very few slices (2-3) and the part-to-whole relationship is the primary message.
  • Scatter plots over bubble charts: For bivariate relationships, scatter plots (position only) are more accurately read than bubble charts (position + area). Add the area channel only when a third variable genuinely requires encoding.
  • Tables vs charts: For precise lookup of individual values, a well-formatted table may outperform a chart. Charts excel at revealing patterns, trends, and comparisons; tables excel at precise retrieval.
Reading: Few (2009), Ch. 3-5; Kirk (2019), Ch. 7.
3

Pillar 3: Design Principles and Techniques

Primary LO: LO5 | Indicative Content: Topics 3, 8, 9

This pillar translates perceptual science into actionable design principles. LO5 asks for critical evaluation and application of design principles and interaction strategies. Mastery here requires not just knowing the principles but being able to justify design choices by reference to perception, cognition, and communication goals.

3.1

Tufte's Principles

3.1.1

Data-Ink Ratio

  • Definition: The proportion of a graphic's ink devoted to the non-redundant display of data information. Data-ink ratio = data-ink / total ink used. A ratio of 1.0 means every mark on the chart is data; no decorative or redundant elements are present.
  • The principle: Within reason, maximise the data-ink ratio. Remove every element that does not directly convey data.
  • Application: Remove unnecessary gridlines, borders, background fills, decorative images, and redundant labels. Simplify axis markings. Eliminate 3D effects that serve no analytical purpose.
  • Critical perspective: Tufte's principle is a guideline, not an absolute rule. Some "non-data ink" (titles, annotations, axis labels, legends) is essential for comprehension. The principle warns against chartjunk, not against contextual elements.
Reading: Tufte (2001), Ch. 6; Kirk (2019), Ch. 8; Few (2009), Ch. 3.
3.1.2

Chartjunk

  • Definition: Visual elements in charts that do not convey information and that distract from the data. Coined by Tufte.
  • Examples: Moiré patterns, heavy gridlines, decorative illustrations ("ducks"), gratuitous use of colour gradients, unnecessary 3D rendering.
  • The debate: Tufte argues chartjunk always detracts. Some researchers (Bateman et al., 2010) found that "chart junk" can improve memorability. The professional consensus remains with Tufte for analytical contexts, while acknowledging that engaging design has value in public-facing communication.
Reading: Tufte (2001), Ch. 5-6.
3.1.3

Lie Factor and Graphical Integrity

  • Lie Factor formula: Lie Factor = (size of effect shown in graphic) / (size of effect in data). A Lie Factor of 1.0 is accurate. Values below 0.95 or above 1.05 indicate meaningful distortion.
  • Graphical integrity principles (Tufte):
    • Representation of numbers should be directly proportional to the numerical quantities represented.
    • Use clear, detailed, and thorough labelling.
    • Show data variation, not design variation.
    • In time series, standardise monetary units for inflation.
    • The number of information-carrying dimensions should not exceed the number of dimensions in the data.
    • Graphics must not quote data out of context.
Reading: Tufte (2001), Ch. 2.
3.1.4

Small Multiples and Sparklines

  • Small multiples: A series of similar charts, each showing a different slice of the data (different time period, category, or condition), arranged in a grid. The consistent format enables rapid comparison because the viewer learns the encoding once and applies it repeatedly.
  • Sparklines: Intense, simple, word-sized graphics embedded in text or tables. Typically used for time series. They sacrifice axis detail for compactness and contextual placement.
Reading: Tufte (2001), Ch. 8; Few (2009), Ch. 6.

3.3

Visual Hierarchy and Layout

3.3.1

Reading Patterns

  • F-pattern: In Western cultures, viewers scan content in an F-shape: across the top, then down the left side, then selectively across again. Place the most important visualization or summary metric at the top-left.
  • Z-pattern: For simpler layouts (landing pages, single-chart views), the eye follows a Z: top-left to top-right, then diagonally to bottom-left, then across to bottom-right.
  • Design implication: The top-left quadrant of any dashboard or report page is the highest-attention zone. Use it for the most critical information.
Reading: Kirk (2019), Ch. 9; Knaflic (2015), Ch. 5.
3.3.2

Visual Weight and Emphasis

  • Visual weight: The perceived importance of an element, determined by size, colour intensity, contrast with surroundings, and isolation (whitespace).
  • Creating emphasis: Make the most important data visually heaviest (larger, brighter, higher contrast). De-emphasise supporting elements (lighter colours, thinner lines, smaller fonts).
  • Strategic use of colour: Use a restrained palette and reserve saturated or warm colours for elements that demand attention. Grey is the designer's best friend for de-emphasising context.
Reading: Knaflic (2015), Ch. 4; Kirk (2019), Ch. 8.
3.3.3

Whitespace and Grid Systems

  • Whitespace (negative space): The empty space between and around visual elements. Not wasted space; it provides visual breathing room, separates groups, and reduces cognitive load.
  • Grid systems: Underlying grid structures create alignment and consistency across multi-chart dashboards. Elements that share grid lines appear more organised and professional.
  • The temptation to fill: Novice designers fill every available pixel. Expert designers know that whitespace is a design element, not an absence of design.
Reading: Kirk (2019), Ch. 9; Few (2009), Ch. 6.
3.4

Typography, Labelling, and Annotation

3.4.1

Font Choices

  • Sans-serif fonts for screens: Fonts like Segoe UI, Arial, Helvetica, and Calibri are optimised for screen readability at small sizes.
  • Hierarchy through typography: Use font weight (bold), size, and sometimes colour to create a clear typographic hierarchy (title > subtitle > axis labels > data labels > footnotes).
  • Avoid decorative fonts: In analytical visualization, legibility always trumps style.
Reading: Kirk (2019), Ch. 8; Knaflic (2015), Ch. 5.
3.4.2

Direct Labelling vs Legends

  • Direct labelling: Placing labels directly on or next to data elements (bars, lines, data points). Eliminates the cognitive cost of looking back and forth between the chart and a separate legend.
  • When legends are necessary: When direct labelling would create clutter (many overlapping series) or when interactive tooltips serve the purpose.
  • Best practice: Default to direct labelling. Use a legend only when direct labelling is impractical.
Reading: Knaflic (2015), Ch. 4; Few (2009), Ch. 4.
3.4.3

Annotation Strategies

  • Purpose of annotation: To guide the viewer's attention, provide context, explain anomalies, and bridge the gap between data and insight.
  • Types: Callout boxes, reference lines, shaded regions (e.g., recession periods), arrows pointing to notable data points, explanatory text.
  • The annotation principle: Annotate what matters. Every annotation should answer a question the viewer is likely to ask or highlight something they might otherwise miss.
Reading: Knaflic (2015), Ch. 4, 8; Kirk (2019), Ch. 8.
3.5

Colour Strategy in Practice

3.5.1

Choosing Palettes

  • Start with purpose: The palette should serve the data and the task. Sequential data needs a sequential palette; categorical data needs distinct hues; diverging data needs a diverging palette.
  • Limit the palette: Use the minimum number of colours necessary. More colours does not mean more information; it often means more confusion.
  • Tools: ColorBrewer (colorbrewer2.org), Viridis family, Adobe Color, Coolors. These tools ensure palettes are perceptually sound and accessible.
Reading: Kirk (2019), Ch. 8; Ware (2012), Ch. 4.
3.5.2

Accessibility in Colour Design

  • Always provide redundant encoding: Shape, pattern, or direct labels in addition to colour.
  • Test with simulation tools: View your visualization as a person with deuteranopia, protanopia, and tritanopia would see it.
  • High-contrast defaults: Ensure sufficient contrast between data elements and between elements and background.
Reading: Few (2009), Ch. 4; Ware (2012), Ch. 4.
3.6

Dashboard Design Principles

3.6.1

Shneiderman's Visual Information-Seeking Mantra

  • "Overview first, zoom and filter, then details on demand." This three-step mantra (Ben Shneiderman, 1996) is the foundational principle of interactive visualization design.
    • Overview: Present a high-level summary that gives the user orientation and context.
    • Zoom and filter: Allow the user to narrow focus to areas of interest.
    • Details on demand: Provide granular information when the user explicitly requests it (click, hover, drill-down).
  • Dashboard application: A dashboard's default view should be the overview. Filters, slicers, and drill-down interactions enable zoom and detail.

Exam Tip

Shneiderman's mantra is a key framework for LO5. Be prepared to explain it and apply it to dashboard design scenarios.

Reading: Ward, Grinstein & Keim (2010), Ch. 12; Kirk (2019), Ch. 10.
3.6.2

Dashboard Layout Best Practices

  • One screen, one story: A dashboard should fit on a single screen without scrolling. If it requires scrolling, it contains too much information for a single view.
  • Logical grouping: Group related metrics using proximity and enclosure. Use consistent colour coding to link related elements across the dashboard.
  • Context and comparison: Always provide context (targets, benchmarks, prior-period values). A number in isolation is meaningless.
  • The 5-second test: If a viewer cannot identify the dashboard's primary message within 5 seconds, the design needs simplification.
Reading: Few (2009), Ch. 6; Knaflic (2015), Ch. 5.
3.7

Storytelling with Data

3.7.1

Knaflic's Framework

  • Three pillars (Knaflic, 2015):
    1. Understand the context: Who is your audience? What do they need to know? What action do you want them to take?
    2. Choose an appropriate visual display: Select the chart type, colour scheme, and layout that best serve the context.
    3. Eliminate clutter: Remove everything that does not directly serve the message (chartjunk, unnecessary labels, excessive colour).
  • Additional steps: Focus attention (use pre-attentive attributes to guide the eye); tell a story (narrative arc); think like a designer (form follows function).
Reading: Knaflic (2015), entire book; Kirk (2019), Ch. 2.
3.7.2

Narrative Arc in Visualization

  • Setup (context): What is the situation? What question are we answering?
  • Rising action (data reveal): What does the data show? Build understanding incrementally.
  • Climax (key insight): The critical finding. The moment of "so what?"
  • Resolution (recommendation): What should be done based on the insight?
  • Application: Sequential slide decks, annotated dashboards, scrollytelling (web-based narratives that reveal data as the user scrolls).
Reading: Knaflic (2015), Ch. 7-8; Steele & Iliinsky (2011), Ch. 3.
3.7.3

Exploratory vs Explanatory Storytelling

  • Exploratory: Open-ended, tool-driven, analyst-facing. The goal is discovery. Interactivity is essential.
  • Explanatory: Message-driven, audience-facing. The goal is communication. The designer has already identified the insight and is guiding the viewer toward it.
  • The mistake: Using an exploratory format (complex, interactive, multi-dimensional) for an explanatory purpose (presenting to executives who need a clear, focused message). Or the reverse: presenting a single static chart when the audience needs to explore.
Reading: Knaflic (2015), Ch. 1; Kirk (2019), Ch. 2.
4

Pillar 4: Advanced Visualization Techniques

Primary LOs: LO1, LO5 | Indicative Content: Topic 4

This pillar covers specialised visualization techniques for data types that go beyond simple categorical and temporal comparison. It builds on the foundations of Pillars 1-3 and tests deeper understanding of data representation.

4.1

Time Series Visualization

4.1.1

Line Charts and Temporal Patterns

  • The default for time series: Line charts encode time on the x-axis and a quantitative variable on the y-axis. The line itself exploits the Gestalt principle of continuity, making trends and patterns immediately visible.
  • Temporal patterns to identify: Trend (long-term direction), seasonality (repeating patterns at fixed intervals), cyclicality (repeating patterns at variable intervals), noise (random variation).
  • Multiple series: Overlaying multiple lines enables comparison but creates clutter beyond 4-5 series. Use colour coding, direct labelling, and possibly small multiples instead.
Reading: Kirk (2019), Ch. 6; Ward, Grinstein & Keim (2010), Ch. 8; Few (2009), Ch. 5.
4.1.2

Sparklines and Horizon Charts

  • Sparklines (Tufte): Miniature time-series charts, typically without axes, embedded in text or table cells. Ideal for showing trends at a glance across many items (e.g., stock price sparklines for a portfolio of 50 equities).
  • Horizon charts: A space-efficient technique that layers a time-series chart by folding the y-axis and using colour intensity to represent magnitude. Counterintuitive at first but highly effective for comparing many time series in limited vertical space.
Reading: Tufte (2001), Ch. 8 (sparklines); Ward, Grinstein & Keim (2010), Ch. 8.
4.1.3

Animated and Interactive Time Series

  • Animation: Stepping through time can reveal dynamics that static views miss (Hans Rosling's Gapminder is the canonical example).
  • Interactive scrubbing: Allowing users to move through time with a slider or play/pause control.
  • Caution: Animation can be compelling but also fleeting. Viewers cannot compare frames they can no longer see. Always provide a static summary view alongside animation.
Reading: Kirk (2019), Ch. 10; Ward, Grinstein & Keim (2010), Ch. 12.
4.2

High-dimensional Data

4.2.1

Parallel Coordinates

  • How it works: Each variable is represented as a vertical axis. Observations are drawn as lines connecting their values across all axes. Patterns, clusters, and outliers become visible as bundles of lines or isolated strands.
  • Strengths: Can display many variables simultaneously (10-20+). Effective for identifying correlations, clusters, and trade-offs.
  • Weaknesses: Becomes cluttered with many observations (overplotting). Axis ordering affects which patterns are visible. Requires interaction (brushing, filtering) to be effective at scale.
Reading: Ward, Grinstein & Keim (2010), Ch. 9; Kirk (2019), Ch. 6.
4.2.2

Star Plots (Radar Charts)

  • How it works: Variables are arranged as spokes radiating from a central point. Each observation creates a polygon connecting its values on each spoke.
  • Use case: Comparing a small number of observations across multiple variables (e.g., comparing two products across 6 quality dimensions).
  • Weaknesses: Hard to read with many observations; area encoding is perceptually unreliable (Cleveland and McGill); axis ordering affects the shape and therefore the perceived pattern.
Reading: Ward, Grinstein & Keim (2010), Ch. 9; Kirk (2019), Ch. 6.
4.2.3

Dimensionality Reduction Concepts

  • The problem: Human visual perception is limited to 2-3 spatial dimensions. Datasets with dozens or hundreds of variables cannot be directly visualized.
  • PCA (Principal Component Analysis): Reduces dimensions by finding the axes of greatest variance. The first two principal components can be plotted as a 2D scatter plot.
  • t-SNE and UMAP: Non-linear dimensionality reduction techniques that preserve local structure, useful for revealing clusters in high-dimensional data.
  • Designer's role: Understand what these techniques do conceptually (projecting high-dimensional data into viewable dimensions) even if the mathematical details are beyond the module scope. The resulting 2D plot must be interpreted with care; distances may not be meaningful.
Reading: Ward, Grinstein & Keim (2010), Ch. 9; Ware (2012), Ch. 10.
4.3

Network and Graph Visualization

4.3.1

Node-Link Diagrams

  • Definition: Entities are represented as nodes (circles or other shapes); relationships are represented as edges (lines connecting nodes).
  • Force-directed layouts: Algorithms (e.g., Fruchterman-Reingold) that position nodes to minimise edge crossings and create visually coherent layouts. Nodes connected by edges are pulled together; unconnected nodes are pushed apart.
  • Use cases: Social networks, organisational charts, supply chain relationships, citation networks.
  • Scalability challenge: Node-link diagrams become illegible ("hairball" problem) with more than a few hundred nodes and edges. Requires interaction (filtering, zooming, highlighting) or alternative representations.
Reading: Ward, Grinstein & Keim (2010), Ch. 11; Kirk (2019), Ch. 6.
4.3.2

Adjacency Matrices

  • Definition: A matrix representation of a network. Rows and columns represent nodes; cells are filled (or coloured) to indicate connections.
  • Advantages over node-link: No edge-crossing problem; scales better for dense networks; exact connection lookup is straightforward.
  • Disadvantages: Less intuitive; path-following (tracing connections across multiple hops) is much harder than in node-link diagrams.
  • When to use each: Node-link for sparse networks and path-tracing tasks; adjacency matrices for dense networks and connection lookup tasks.
Reading: Ward, Grinstein & Keim (2010), Ch. 11.
4.4

Spatial and Geographic Visualization

4.4.1

Choropleth Maps

  • Definition: Thematic maps in which geographic areas (countries, states, counties) are shaded according to a quantitative variable.
  • Design considerations: Use sequential colour scales for sequential data; diverging scales for data with a meaningful midpoint. Normalise data by population or area to avoid the large-area-dominates-perception problem.
  • Limitation: Choropleth maps give visual weight to geographically large but potentially sparsely populated regions, potentially distorting the viewer's perception of importance.
Reading: Kirk (2019), Ch. 6; Ward, Grinstein & Keim (2010), Ch. 10.
4.4.2

Cartograms and Proportional Symbol Maps

  • Cartograms: Distort geographic area to be proportional to a variable of interest (e.g., each country sized by population rather than land area). Effective for correcting the choropleth bias but can be disorienting.
  • Proportional symbol maps: Place symbols (circles, squares) at geographic locations, sized proportionally to a variable. Avoids area distortion but introduces the area-perception problem (Stevens' power law).
Reading: Ward, Grinstein & Keim (2010), Ch. 10; Kirk (2019), Ch. 6.
4.5

Part-to-Whole and Hierarchical Data

4.5.1

Treemaps

  • Definition: A space-filling visualization of hierarchical data. Each branch of the hierarchy is represented as a rectangle, subdivided into smaller rectangles for sub-branches. Size encodes a quantitative variable (e.g., revenue, file size).
  • Strengths: Excellent use of space; can display thousands of leaf nodes; reveals relative proportions and hierarchical structure simultaneously.
  • Weaknesses: Hard to compare rectangles of similar but not identical size (area encoding is perceptually imprecise); deep hierarchies are difficult to navigate without interaction.
Reading: Ward, Grinstein & Keim (2010), Ch. 11; Kirk (2019), Ch. 6.
4.5.2

Sunburst and Icicle Plots

  • Sunburst diagrams: Radial representation of hierarchical data. The root is at the centre; each ring outward represents a level in the hierarchy. Angular extent encodes proportion.
  • Icicle plots: A rectangular, top-down alternative to sunburst. Each level is a horizontal strip, subdivided by category. Width encodes proportion.
  • Trade-offs: Sunburst diagrams are visually appealing but use angle and area encoding (less accurate). Icicle plots use length (more accurate) but are less visually engaging.
Reading: Ward, Grinstein & Keim (2010), Ch. 11; Kirk (2019), Ch. 6.
5

Pillar 5: Interactivity and Tools

Primary LO: LO5 | Supporting LOs: LO3, LO4 | Indicative Content: Topics 6, 7, 8

Interactivity transforms visualization from a static product into a dynamic medium for exploration and discovery. This pillar covers the principles, techniques, and tools of interactive visualization, with particular emphasis on Power BI as the module's industry-standard platform.

5.1

Why Interactivity Matters

5.1.1

Exploration vs Explanation

  • Exploration: The user does not yet know what the data contains. Interactivity enables them to ask questions, test hypotheses, and discover patterns. The user drives the inquiry.
  • Explanation: The designer has already identified the insight. Interactivity is used to allow the viewer to explore supporting detail without cluttering the primary message.
  • Design implication: Exploratory visualizations need rich interaction (filtering, brushing, multiple views). Explanatory visualizations need targeted interaction (tooltips, optional drill-down) that does not distract from the narrative.
Reading: Kirk (2019), Ch. 10; Ward, Grinstein & Keim (2010), Ch. 12.
5.1.2

User Agency and Engagement

  • Psychological ownership: When users can interact with data, they develop a sense of ownership over the insights they discover. This increases engagement and the likelihood that findings will be acted upon.
  • Trust through transparency: Interactivity allows users to "check the data" by drilling down, filtering, and cross-referencing. This builds trust in the visualization and its conclusions.
  • The balance: Too little interactivity produces passive consumption; too much creates complexity that overwhelms. The right level of interactivity matches the audience's skill and motivation.
Reading: Kirk (2019), Ch. 10; Steele & Iliinsky (2011), Ch. 5.
5.1.3

Shneiderman's Information-Seeking Mantra Revisited

  • Application to interactivity: The mantra ("Overview first, zoom and filter, then details on demand") is not just a design guideline; it is a model of how users naturally seek information. Effective interactive design mirrors this cognitive process.
  • Three levels of interaction:
    1. Overview interactions: Reset to default view, toggle between summary and detail mode.
    2. Zoom and filter interactions: Slicers, range selectors, category filters, geographic zoom.
    3. Detail interactions: Tooltips, click-to-expand, drill-through to underlying data.
Reading: Ward, Grinstein & Keim (2010), Ch. 12.
5.2

Interaction Techniques

5.2.1

Filtering and Selection

  • Filtering: Removing data from view that does not meet specified criteria (e.g., show only sales from Region A; show only dates after 2023).
  • Dynamic queries: Filters that update the visualization in real time as the user adjusts parameters (sliders for ranges, checkboxes for categories).
  • Visual filtering (slicers): Dashboard components that allow users to filter all linked visualizations simultaneously by clicking on category buttons or list items.
Reading: Kirk (2019), Ch. 10; Ward, Grinstein & Keim (2010), Ch. 12.
5.2.2

Brushing and Linking

  • Brushing: Selecting a subset of data in one view (e.g., highlighting a cluster in a scatter plot). The selection is visually marked (highlighted, coloured) while unselected data is de-emphasised.
  • Linking: The brushed selection is simultaneously highlighted in all other linked views (e.g., the same observations highlighted in a parallel coordinates plot, a map, and a bar chart).
  • Power: Brushing and linking enables multivariate exploration across multiple coordinated views. A pattern visible in one representation can be investigated in another.
Reading: Ward, Grinstein & Keim (2010), Ch. 12; Ware (2012), Ch. 11.
5.2.3

Zooming, Panning, and Drill-Down

  • Geometric zoom: Magnifying a region of the visualization (e.g., zooming into a section of a scatter plot or map).
  • Semantic zoom: Changing the level of detail as the user zooms in. At the overview level, show aggregated data; at the detail level, show individual records.
  • Drill-down: Moving from a summary level to a more detailed level within a data hierarchy (e.g., from national sales to regional to city to individual store). The inverse operation is roll-up.
Reading: Kirk (2019), Ch. 10; Ward, Grinstein & Keim (2010), Ch. 12.
5.2.4

Tooltips and Cross-Filtering

  • Tooltips: Pop-up displays that appear when the user hovers over a data element, showing detailed information (exact values, labels, additional context) without cluttering the main view.
  • Cross-filtering: Clicking or selecting an element in one visualization automatically filters all other visualizations on the same page. A core feature of Power BI dashboards.
  • Design principles for tooltips: Keep them concise; include the metric name, value, and one or two contextual data points; avoid overloading with information.
Reading: Kirk (2019), Ch. 10; Few (2009), Ch. 6.
5.3

Progressive Disclosure

5.3.1

Layered Information Architecture

  • The principle: Present the most important information first, with options to access deeper layers of detail. Do not force the viewer to process everything at once.
  • Implementation patterns:
    • Overview + detail: A summary view with the option to click through to detail pages.
    • Focus + context: Showing a detailed view of a selected subset while maintaining a peripheral view of the whole (e.g., a magnified region within a minimap).
    • Drill-through pages: In Power BI, right-clicking on a data point opens a separate page with detailed analysis specific to that selection.
Reading: Ward, Grinstein & Keim (2010), Ch. 12; Knaflic (2015), Ch. 5.
5.3.2

When Less Interaction is More

  • Over-interaction risk: Providing too many interactive options can overwhelm users, especially non-technical audiences. If every element is clickable and every dimension is filterable, the user does not know where to start.
  • Guided interaction: For explanatory visualizations, constrain interaction to a predefined set of meaningful paths. Guide the user through the story rather than setting them loose in a data playground.
Reading: Knaflic (2015), Ch. 5; Kirk (2019), Ch. 10.
5.4

Power BI as a Visualization Platform

5.4.1

Core Concepts

  • Components: Power BI Desktop (authoring), Power BI Service (cloud sharing and collaboration), Power BI Mobile (consumption on devices).
  • Data model: Tables connected by relationships. The underlying model is a star schema: fact tables (transactions, events) surrounded by dimension tables (dates, products, regions).
  • Visuals: Built-in chart types plus a marketplace of custom visuals. Each visual is connected to the data model and responds to slicers and cross-filters.
  • Pages and reports: A report is a collection of pages; each page contains one or more visuals, slicers, and text elements.
Reading: Kirk (2019), Ch. 10 (tool context); module practical materials.
5.4.2

DAX Basics

  • DAX (Data Analysis Expressions): A formula language used in Power BI for creating calculated columns, measures, and tables.
  • Key concepts: Measures (aggregate calculations that respond to filter context, e.g., SUM, AVERAGE, COUNTROWS), calculated columns (row-level computations stored in the data model), filter context (the set of filters active when a measure is evaluated).
  • Common DAX patterns: Year-over-year growth, running totals, percentage of parent, dynamic ranking, time intelligence functions (TOTALYTD, SAMEPERIODLASTYEAR).
  • Why it matters for visualization: DAX enables the creation of dynamic, context-sensitive metrics that respond to user interaction. Without DAX, dashboards are limited to simple aggregations.
Reading: Module practical materials; Barker (2013).
5.4.3

Custom Visuals and the Power BI Ecosystem

  • Custom visuals: Extend Power BI beyond built-in chart types. Examples include Chiclet slicers, advanced card visuals, Sankey diagrams, and network graphs.
  • AppSource marketplace: A curated library of certified and community-created custom visuals.
  • When to use custom visuals: When the built-in visuals cannot represent the required data type or interaction pattern. Use cautiously; custom visuals may have performance implications and certification concerns.
  • Sharing and collaboration: Workspaces, apps, row-level security (RLS), and scheduled refresh enable organisational deployment and governance.
Reading: Module practical materials; Barker (2013).
5.5

Presentation and Communication

5.5.1

Adjusting for Audience

  • Technical audience: Can handle complexity, multiple views, and rich interaction. Provide tools for exploration.
  • Executive audience: Needs a clear, focused message. Limit the number of visuals. Lead with the headline finding. Provide detail on demand but do not require it for comprehension.
  • General public: Requires maximum simplicity. Avoid jargon, use familiar chart types, annotate heavily, and test for accessibility.
Reading: Knaflic (2015), Ch. 1, 3; Kirk (2019), Ch. 2.
5.5.2

Live Dashboards vs Static Reports

  • Live dashboards: Updated in real time or near-real time. Suited for operational monitoring (call centre metrics, sales pipeline, website analytics). Risk: information overload if not well-designed.
  • Static reports: Fixed-point-in-time documents. Suited for periodic review (monthly board reports, quarterly business reviews). Advantage: can be carefully crafted for narrative impact.
  • The hybrid approach: A live dashboard for day-to-day monitoring, supplemented by periodic narrative reports that provide context, interpretation, and recommendations.
Reading: Few (2009), Ch. 6; Knaflic (2015), Ch. 7.
6

Pillar 6: Evaluation, Critique, and Real-world Application

Primary LOs: LO5, LO6 | Indicative Content: Topics 5, 9, 10

This pillar brings together everything from the preceding pillars into a framework for critically evaluating visualizations and proposing designs for specific contexts. LO6 explicitly asks students to propose suitable visualization designs for particular data characteristics and applications.

6.1

Evaluating Visualizations

6.1.1

Heuristic Evaluation

  • What it is: Expert review of a visualization against a set of established design heuristics (principles).
  • Relevant heuristic sets:
    • Tufte's principles (data-ink ratio, graphical integrity)
    • Cleveland and McGill's encoding accuracy hierarchy
    • Shneiderman's mantra (overview, zoom and filter, details on demand)
    • Gestalt principles (is grouping used effectively?)
    • Colour and accessibility (is it colour-blind safe? are contrast ratios adequate?)
  • Process: Systematically check each heuristic against the visualization. Document violations and propose specific remedies.
Reading: Kirk (2019), Ch. 11; Ward, Grinstein & Keim (2010), Ch. 13.
6.1.2

Usability Testing

  • What it is: Observing representative users as they attempt to extract information or complete tasks using the visualization.
  • Metrics: Task completion rate, time to insight, error rate, subjective satisfaction.
  • Think-aloud protocol: Users verbalise their thought process while interacting with the visualization, revealing confusion, misinterpretation, and points of friction.
  • When to use: When the audience includes non-expert users; when the visualization will be widely deployed; when the stakes of misinterpretation are high.
Reading: Ward, Grinstein & Keim (2010), Ch. 13; Ware (2012), Ch. 12.
6.1.3

Cognitive Walkthroughs

  • What it is: The evaluator steps through a sequence of tasks from the user's perspective, asking at each step: "Will the user know what to do? Will they understand the feedback the system provides?"
  • Application to visualization: Walk through the intended information-seeking path. Does the viewer know where to look first? Can they find the comparison they need? Do interactive elements have clear affordances?
Reading: Ward, Grinstein & Keim (2010), Ch. 13.
6.2

Common Visualization Failures

6.2.1

Truncated Axes

  • The problem: Starting a bar chart's y-axis at a value other than zero exaggerates differences between bars, making small variations appear dramatic.
  • When it might be acceptable: Line charts (which emphasise change rather than absolute magnitude) can sometimes justify non-zero baselines, provided the intent is clearly communicated.
Reading: Tufte (2001), Ch. 2; Few (2009), Ch. 3.
6.2.2

Rainbow Colour Palettes

  • The problem: Rainbow (spectral) palettes are perceptually non-uniform (some colour transitions appear larger than others), have no inherent ordering, and fail for colour-blind viewers.
  • The fix: Use sequential palettes (e.g., Viridis) for ordered data. Use categorical palettes with distinct hues for unordered data.
Reading: Ware (2012), Ch. 4; Kirk (2019), Ch. 8.
6.2.3

3D Distortion

  • The problem: 3D bar charts, 3D pie charts, and other 3D effects introduce perspective distortion that makes accurate comparison impossible. Elements in the foreground appear larger; elements in the background appear smaller.
  • The rule: Never use 3D in analytical visualization unless the data is inherently three-dimensional (e.g., 3D scatter plots of spatial data).
Reading: Tufte (2001), Ch. 5; Few (2009), Ch. 3.
6.2.4

Dual Axes Abuse

  • The problem: Placing two y-axes with different scales on the same chart can create the illusion of correlation or causation between unrelated variables. The relationship shown depends entirely on the arbitrary choice of scales.
  • Alternative: Use small multiples or indexed values (rebased to 100) on a single axis.
Reading: Few (2009), Ch. 3; Kirk (2019), Ch. 8.
6.2.5

Overplotting

  • The problem: When too many data points overlap in a scatter plot or too many lines overlap in a multi-series line chart, individual observations become indistinguishable.
  • Solutions: Transparency (alpha blending), jittering, binning/hexbin plots, sampling, small multiples, density contour plots.
Reading: Kirk (2019), Ch. 6; Ward, Grinstein & Keim (2010), Ch. 5.
6.3

Tailoring for Audience and Context

6.3.1

Expert vs Novice Audiences

  • Expert audiences: Can handle complex encodings (parallel coordinates, small multiples, network diagrams). Expect precision and comprehensive data access. Interaction-rich designs are appropriate.
  • Novice audiences: Need familiar chart types (bar, line, pie), heavy annotation, limited interaction, and clear labelling. Minimise cognitive load.
  • The designer's challenge: Resisting the temptation to design for fellow data professionals when the actual audience is non-technical stakeholders.
Reading: Kirk (2019), Ch. 2; Knaflic (2015), Ch. 1.
6.3.2

Presentation vs Exploration

  • Presentation mode: The visualization tells a predetermined story. Design is tightly controlled. The viewer follows the designer's narrative arc. Annotations, sequencing, and selective highlighting guide interpretation.
  • Exploration mode: The visualization is a tool for discovery. The user controls the view. Rich interactivity, multiple linked views, and flexible filtering are essential.
  • Matching mode to context: Board presentations require presentation mode. Analyst workbenches require exploration mode. Self-service business user tools need a hybrid approach.
Reading: Knaflic (2015), Ch. 1; Kirk (2019), Ch. 2, 10.
6.3.3

Print vs Screen

  • Print constraints: No interactivity; fixed resolution; limited colour range. Requires that the visualization be self-contained and fully annotated.
  • Screen advantages: Interactivity, animation, dynamic updates, colour gamut. But also: distraction, varying screen sizes, and the assumption that tooltips are available.
  • Responsive design: Adapting visualizations for different screen sizes (desktop, tablet, mobile). In Power BI, this means creating separate mobile layouts.
Reading: Kirk (2019), Ch. 9; Few (2009), Ch. 6.
6.4

Iterative Design and Feedback

6.4.1

Storyboarding

  • What it is: Sketching the sequence of views or pages before building anything in a tool. Captures the narrative flow, key data points to highlight, and interaction patterns.
  • Why it matters: Investing time in storyboarding before opening Power BI (or any tool) prevents costly redesigns and ensures the visualization serves its communicative purpose.
  • Process: Define the audience and message; sketch rough layouts on paper or whiteboard; sequence the views; identify data requirements; get feedback before building.
Reading: Knaflic (2015), Ch. 7; Kirk (2019), Ch. 3.
6.4.2

Prototyping and Critique Cycles

  • Rapid prototyping: Build a quick, low-fidelity version to test with users. Use paper sketches, wireframes, or quick Power BI drafts.
  • Critique frameworks: Structured feedback sessions where reviewers evaluate the prototype against specific criteria (clarity, accuracy, audience appropriateness, design principles).
  • Iteration: Incorporate feedback, revise, and test again. The best visualizations are rarely produced in a single pass.
Reading: Kirk (2019), Ch. 3; Steele & Iliinsky (2011), Ch. 4.
6.4.3

User Testing and Feedback Loops

  • Task-based testing: Give users specific tasks (e.g., "What was the highest-selling product in Q3?") and observe whether the visualization enables them to answer quickly and accurately.
  • Feedback from stakeholders: Ask "What questions does this answer for you? What questions does it leave unanswered? Is anything confusing?" These open-ended questions reveal design gaps that the designer cannot see.
  • Continuous improvement: Deployed dashboards should be periodically reviewed and updated based on usage patterns and user feedback.

Exam Tip

Iterative design and evaluation methodology are examinable under LO5. Be prepared to describe and justify a design process, not just a design outcome.

Reading: Knaflic (2015), Ch. 9; Ward, Grinstein & Keim (2010), Ch. 13.
6.5

Real-world Applications

6.5.1

Business Dashboards

  • Use cases: Sales performance, financial reporting, marketing analytics, supply chain monitoring, customer analytics.
  • Key design challenge: Balancing comprehensiveness with clarity. Business dashboards often try to show everything and end up communicating nothing.
  • Best practice: Design for the decision, not for the data. Ask "What decision will this dashboard support?" and include only the information necessary for that decision.
Reading: Few (2009), Ch. 6; Knaflic (2015), Ch. 5.
6.5.2

Healthcare Visualization

  • Use cases: Patient outcome tracking, epidemiological mapping (cf. John Snow), clinical trial results, hospital performance metrics.
  • Ethical considerations: Privacy (GDPR, HIPAA), accuracy (clinical decisions depend on data quality), accessibility (medical professionals may not be visualization experts).
Reading: Kirk (2019), Ch. 1 (historical context); Ward, Grinstein & Keim (2010), Ch. 10.
6.5.3

Journalism and Public Communication

  • Data journalism: News organisations use visualization to make complex topics (elections, budgets, pandemics) accessible to the public.
  • The scrollytelling format: Web-based narratives that reveal data progressively as the user scrolls. Examples include The New York Times, The Guardian, and The Pudding.
  • Trust and transparency: Journalism requires showing methodology and data sources. Interactive visualizations that allow the public to explore the underlying data build credibility.
Reading: Steele & Iliinsky (2011), Ch. 1-3; Kirk (2019), Ch. 1.
6.5.4

Spatial Visualization and Public Policy

  • Urban planning: Visualizing population density, transport networks, pollution levels, and land use to support planning decisions.
  • Disaster response: Real-time mapping of natural disasters, resource allocation, and evacuation routes.
  • Election mapping: Choropleth maps of voting patterns; the challenges of geographic representation (large rural areas vs dense urban areas).
Reading: Ward, Grinstein & Keim (2010), Ch. 10; Kirk (2019), Ch. 6.
6.5.5

Scientific Visualization

  • Domain-specific examples: Molecular structures, climate models, fluid dynamics, brain imaging.
  • Characteristics: Often 3D; highly technical audiences; precision and accuracy paramount; interactivity essential for exploration.
  • Connection to this module: While scientific visualization is a specialised field, the foundational principles (perception, design, evaluation) are shared with information visualization.
Reading: Ward, Grinstein & Keim (2010), Ch. 10; Ware (2012), Ch. 10.

Core Concepts for Revision

Based on the indicative content and learning outcomes, the following topics are most likely to appear in some form:

  1. Pre-attentive attributes and their application: Be prepared to identify attributes in a given visualisation and evaluate their effectiveness. Reference Ware (2012) specifically.
  2. Gestalt principles: Know all six, provide examples of each in visualisation contexts, and be able to critique a dashboard layout using Gestalt theory.
  3. Cleveland and McGill's hierarchy: Rank encoding channels by accuracy; explain why certain chart types are more effective than others for quantitative comparison.
  4. Tufte's principles (data-ink ratio, chartjunk, graphical integrity, Lie Factor): Be prepared to apply these to a visualisation scenario and evaluate whether a given chart adheres to or violates them.
  5. Cognitive load and working memory: Explain how poor design increases extraneous load; propose design improvements that reduce cognitive burden.
  6. Exploratory vs. explanatory visualisation: Define, distinguish, and explain the design implications of each. Reference Knaflic (2015).
  7. Shneiderman's mantra: State it, explain it, and evaluate a visualisation or dashboard against it.
  8. Visual storytelling: Describe the elements of effective visual storytelling; distinguish between a chart and a story; reference Knaflic (2015).
  9. Ethics of data visualisation: Identify common deceptive practices; explain the designer's ethical responsibilities; reference Tufte and Kirk.
  10. Chart type selection: Given a dataset and a communicative goal, justify the selection of an appropriate chart type using design principles and perceptual theory.

Exam Answer Strategy

  • Long-form questions: Structure your answer with an introduction, 3-4 well-developed points (each with theory, example, and critical evaluation), and a brief conclusion. Reference the recommended texts explicitly by author and year.
  • Short-form questions: Be precise. Define the concept, give one clear example, and note one critical consideration or limitation.
  • Demonstrate reading: The exam rubric rewards evidence of engagement with the literature. Reference Kirk (2019), Knaflic (2015), Ware (2012), Tufte (2001), Few (2009), and Ward et al. (2010) by name. Use specific concepts and terminology from the texts.
  • Be critical, not just descriptive: At Level 9, you are expected to evaluate, critique, and apply, not merely describe. For every concept, be prepared to discuss its strengths, limitations, and the conditions under which it does or does not apply. For example, do not just define chartjunk; discuss the Bateman et al. counter-argument and take a position.
  • Use examples: Concrete examples (from the course, from public data, from business practice) demonstrate application and deepen your argument.
  • Connect theory to practice: The strongest answers link perceptual science (Pillar 2) to design principles (Pillar 3) to practical application (Pillars 5-6). For example, explain why bar charts outperform pie charts by referencing Cleveland and McGill, then discuss when a pie chart might still be acceptable.
