05 What to Revise: LO1 / LO2 / LO5 Map ▶

LO1: Theory & Foundations

  • Purpose of visualisation: explanatory vs exploratory
  • Kirk's design workflow (formulating brief to production)
  • Knaflic: storytelling with data
  • Tufte: data-ink ratio, chartjunk, small multiples
  • Few: dashboards as at-a-glance monitoring
  • Shneiderman mantra: overview, zoom, filter, details-on-demand

LO2: Perception & Cognition

  • Pre-attentive attributes (position, length, hue, motion)
  • Cleveland and McGill: ranking of encodings
  • Gestalt principles (proximity, similarity, closure)
  • Ware: visual thinking and working memory limits
  • Colour: sequential, diverging, categorical; CVD safety
  • Cognitive load and chart clutter

LO5: Design Principles & Integrity

  • Truncated axes, dual axes, log scale (when each is honest)
  • 3D and pie chart pitfalls; when 100% stacked misleads
  • Annotation, hierarchy, focal point
  • Audience analysis: executive vs operational vs analyst
  • Accessibility: contrast, alt-text, keyboard navigation
  • Ethics: AI-generated charts, sparse data, equity in coverage

115 to 120 minSweep: numbering, every sub-part addressed, sources cited.
Source naming wins marks. Even one accurate citation per long answer (Kirk 2019; Knaflic 2015; Cleveland and McGill 1984; Ware 2012; Tufte 2001; Few 2009; Shneiderman 1996) lifts an answer from descriptive to academic.
07 Common Failure Modes ▶

Section A pitfalls

  • Listing chart types without explaining why they fit the data
  • Saying "use colour" without naming a palette family or CVD
  • Quoting principles without naming the author

Section B pitfalls

  • Re-describing the scenario instead of analysing it
  • Skipping audience analysis on dashboard questions
  • Treating ethics as an afterthought rather than a structural section
  • One-line "redesign" answers with no encoding rationale
Honesty note about the mock. The mock is calibrated 5 to 10 marks harder than the May paper. A 55-65% on the mock typically corresponds to a 60-69% on the terminal. Use it to find weak topics, not to predict your final grade.
