Theory Primer: "The 7 Lenses"

15 min, main room · Evaluation framework for the full session

Present these as 7 lenses to look through when evaluating ANY visualisation, including their own.

1
Integrity
Tufte: data-ink ratio, graphical integrity, lie factor
Does the visual representation honestly reflect the data?
2
Perception
Ware, Healey: pre-attentive attributes
Will the viewer see the most important thing FIRST?
3
Grouping
Gestalt: proximity, similarity, enclosure, connection
Does the layout help the viewer understand what belongs together?
4
Encoding
Cleveland and McGill: hierarchy of graphical perception
Are you using the most accurate visual channel for the comparison?
5
Audience
Knaflic, Kirk: audience-first design, the Big Idea
Could your target decision-maker act on this in 30 seconds?
6
Disclosure
Shneiderman: overview first, zoom and filter, details on demand
Can the viewer go from summary to detail without being overwhelmed?
7
Ethics
Tufte, Kirk: responsibility of the visualiser
What does your visualisation NOT show, and could that mislead?

"These 7 lenses are the vocabulary of the terminal exam. Every Section B question can be answered by applying 3-4 of these lenses to a scenario. Today, you're applying them to your own work."

1

The Principle Audit

Team20 min Breakout Rooms

Challenge Benefit

Forces teams to audit their own dashboard design against named principles before submission. Catches design flaws early.

Exam Benefit

Practises the exact skill tested in Section B: evaluate a visualisation using named theories.

Post in Teams Chat

TEAM EXERCISE 1: The Principle Audit (20 minutes)

Open your team's current dashboard or design sketch. Using the 7 Lenses framework, audit your own work.

Step 1 (10 min): Go through each of the 7 lenses. For each, write:

  • Score (Strong / Needs Work / Not Yet Addressed)
  • Evidence (one sentence explaining your score)
  • If "Needs Work": what specific change would fix it, and which principle justifies that change?

Use this format in your Teams chat:

TEAM [number]: Principle Audit
1. Integrity: [Strong/Needs Work/Not Yet] - [evidence]
2. Perception: ...
3. Grouping: ...
4. Encoding: ...
5. Audience: ...
6. Disclosure: ...
7. Ethics: ...

TOP PRIORITY FIX: [the one change that would most improve our dashboard]

Step 2 (10 min): Agree on your TOP 3 design changes based on the audit. For each, write one sentence starting with: "We will change [X] because [named principle] tells us that [reason]."

Paste your full audit into the Teams chat before returning to the main room.

Debrief (10 min)

Two teams present. Ask: "Which lens revealed something you hadn't noticed? Which change are you actually going to make?"

2

The Decision-Maker Test

Individual12 min Main Room

Challenge Benefit

Forces each student to articulate their dashboard's "Big Idea" in one sentence, which is exactly what a decision-maker needs.

Exam Benefit

Practises the audience-awareness skill tested in B3 (audience design) and the clarity expected across all Section B answers.

Post in Teams Chat

INDIVIDUAL EXERCISE 2: The Decision-Maker Test (12 minutes)

Imagine your team's target decision-maker (the person whose mind you want to change) opens your dashboard for exactly 30 seconds. They will not click anything. They will not read small text. They will glance, absorb, and close it.

Answer these four questions. Type your answers into the Teams chat.

  1. Who is your decision-maker? (Job title and one sentence about what pressures they face)
  2. The 30-Second Test: In one sentence, what will they take away from your dashboard in 30 seconds? If you cannot write this sentence, your dashboard does not yet have a thesis.
  3. The Knaflic Test: Complete this sentence: "This dashboard argues that [decision-maker] should [action] because [data evidence]."
  4. The Pre-Attentive Test: What is the single most important data point or trend on your dashboard? What pre-attentive attribute (colour, size, position, motion) makes it impossible to miss? If nothing stands out, name the specific change you would make.

You have 12 minutes. Be specific, not generic.

Debrief (8 min)

Read 3 answers aloud (volunteer or call on). Highlight: who had the clearest Big Idea? Who discovered their dashboard doesn't have one yet? That discovery is worth more than a week of building.

3

Cross-Team Tribunal

Paired Teams25 min Breakout Rooms

Challenge Benefit

Teams get external eyes on their work. Fresh perspectives catch blind spots that the building team cannot see.

Exam Benefit

Directly practises the B1/B2/B4 skill: critically evaluate someone else's visualisation using named theories, then propose improvements.

Setup: Pair teams in breakout rooms (Team 1 with Team 2, Team 3 with Team 4, etc.). Each team shares their dashboard (screen share or link).

Post in Teams Chat

CROSS-TEAM EXERCISE 3: The Tribunal (25 minutes)

You are paired with another team. Each team will evaluate the other's dashboard.

Round 1: Team A presents, Team B evaluates (12 min)

Team A: Share your screen. In 2 minutes, explain your dashboard's purpose, audience, and key message. Then stop talking.

Team B: You have 10 minutes. Using the 7 Lenses, identify:

  • 2 strengths (name the principle that makes it work)
  • 2 weaknesses (name the principle being violated and why it matters for the intended audience)
  • 1 specific improvement (what to change, justified by a named principle)

Write your evaluation in this format:

TRIBUNAL: Team [X] evaluating Team [Y]
STRENGTH 1: [what works] - Principle: [name]
STRENGTH 2: [what works] - Principle: [name]
WEAKNESS 1: [problem] - Violates: [principle] - Impact: [why it matters]
WEAKNESS 2: [problem] - Violates: [principle] - Impact: [why it matters]
IMPROVEMENT: [specific change] - Justified by: [principle]

Round 2: Swap roles (12 min)
Team B presents, Team A evaluates. Same format.

Final minute: Each team writes one sentence: "The most useful feedback we received was..."

Paste both evaluations and your reflection into the Teams chat.

Debrief (10 min)

Two pairs report back. Ask: "What surprised you about how another team saw your work?" and "Which named principle appeared most in your feedback?"

4

The Ethics Stress Test

Team15 min Breakout Rooms

Challenge Benefit

Forces teams to confront what their visualisation omits, exaggerates, or implies. The difference between a good dashboard and a responsible one.

Exam Benefit

Directly prepares for the ethics components in B1a (public health ethics), B4a (investor ethics), and the ethical awareness rewarded across all questions.

Post in Teams Chat

TEAM EXERCISE 4: The Ethics Stress Test (15 minutes)

Look at your own dashboard through the eyes of someone who DISAGREES with your message.

Answer these five questions as a team. Be honest; this is where the real learning happens.

  1. What does your visualisation NOT show? Name one important variable or context that is absent from your dashboard. Could its absence mislead your decision-maker?
  2. The Axis Test: Do any of your charts use a truncated axis, a non-linear scale, or a time range that could exaggerate a trend? If yes, is it justified? What would Tufte say?
  3. The Colour Test: Could your colour choices mislead? Are you using red/green in a way that implies good/bad when the data is neutral? Would a colour-blind viewer lose information?
  4. The Omission Test: If a journalist wrote a headline based solely on your dashboard, what would the headline be? Now: is that headline fair to the full picture, or does it cherry-pick?
  5. The "So What" Sentence: Write one sentence that acknowledges a limitation of your visualisation. This is the kind of sentence that earns marks in the terminal exam and earns trust with a real decision-maker.

Format:

TEAM [number]: Ethics Stress Test
1. Missing variable: ...
2. Axis check: ...
3. Colour check: ...
4. Headline test: ...
5. Limitation sentence: ...

Paste into Teams chat.

Debrief (10 min)

Rapid round: each team reads their "limitation sentence" (question 5). Point out: this kind of intellectual honesty is what separates a first-class exam answer from a good one, and a trustworthy dashboard from a flashy one.

5

The Exam Bridge

Individual15 min Main Room

Challenge Benefit

Nothing directly. This is the bridge to the exam.

Exam Benefit

Students write a practice Section B opening paragraph using their own challenge project as the scenario.

Post in Teams Chat

INDIVIDUAL EXERCISE 5: The Exam Bridge (15 minutes)

Imagine your team's dashboard appears as a scenario in the terminal exam. The question reads:

"Critically evaluate the [your team's dashboard topic] visualisation. Consider the perceptual, cognitive, and ethical implications of the design choices, and propose specific improvements grounded in named design principles."

Write TWO paragraphs:

Paragraph 1 (Evaluation): Identify two design strengths and two design weaknesses of your own dashboard. For each, name the specific theory or principle. Write as if you are answering a 20-mark exam question.

Paragraph 2 (Improvements): Propose two specific improvements. Each must reference a named principle and explain why the change would improve the viewer's experience.

This is exam practice using material you already know deeply. Use the vocabulary: Tufte, Gestalt, pre-attentive, Knaflic, Shneiderman, Cleveland and McGill, progressive disclosure, cognitive load.

Paste into Teams chat. You have 15 minutes.

Debrief

No formal debrief needed. Scan responses. If time permits, read one strong example and explain why it would score well.
